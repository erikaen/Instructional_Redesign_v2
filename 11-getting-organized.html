You have a day job. In your free time you fix bikes — for friends, then friends of friends. You’re good at it, and word gets around.

It grew. Enough that when the gear took over your apartment, you rented a small space nearby to work out of. But the repair work is still tangled up with the rest of your life — and this week, that started to cause problems.

What you’ll discover
  • Why a little success forces you to get organized
  • The first line you draw — and what it’s really about
  • How your repair work starts to become its own thing

Three things happened this week. None of them went smoothly.

Three different moments, one realization: it’s too much to keep in your head. If you’re going to get a grip on this, the first step is to write down what you’ve got.

Your stuff is in two places: the rented space you work out of, and your home. You open a spreadsheet to start a tab — Things — and go to fill it.

Drag (or tap) into the Things tab only what belongs to the repair work. Leave everything else where it is. Use if you’re not sure what something is.

Look at what you just did

You pulled things into that box from both places — the laptop and the desk came from home, the cash drawer doesn’t repair anything, and your own commuter bike stayed out even though it was in the space. So the box isn’t “stuff in the space,” and it isn’t “tools.” It’s something else. The next question names it.

You filled the Things tab from both places, and left a lot behind. You drew a line. What is the line actually about?

Which best describes what ended up on the Things tab?
The line you drew

You drew a line: things related to the repair work go in — wherever they sit, whatever their job — and everything else stays out. Not where it is, not what it’s used for, not who “owns” it. Just: is this part of the repair work, or not? Nobody taught you to draw it. You did it because the work had become its own thing, and you needed to see it clearly.

What you discovered
  • A little success made it impossible to keep the repair work in your head.
  • Sorting your stuff, you drew a line — related to the repair work, or not.
  • That line isn’t about where things are or what they’re used for. It’s about what belongs to the work.
The deeper point

The very first thing anyone who runs anything does — from a one-person bike shop to the largest company on earth — is decide what’s inside it and what’s outside. You just did it by hand, sorting a pile of stuff, without being taught. Hold onto that line; everything else builds on it.
