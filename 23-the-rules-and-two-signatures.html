ANNA
“See the shape? Same sections for every LLC. Yours just carries your name, your business, your numbers. Generate it and we’ll read it together.” (You can reopen City Cycle’s example anytime from the button above.)

You read it in your agreement — Section 9, Capital Contributions. It says what you put into the Company. The answer is everything. Schedule A lists it at its existing book amounts. Move it in, and watch the totals.

§9. “The Sole Member contributes the assets and liabilities of the bicycle-repair business, at their existing book amounts, as set forth on Schedule A.”

And Section 17, Liability of the Sole Member: the Company’s debts are the Company’s, not yours. Remember Smith’s claim from 2.1 — the one that came after your savings, your car, even your earnings? Send it again, now that the wall exists.

§17. “The Sole Member is not personally liable for the debts or obligations of the Company.”

A legal body. Rules. Your business inside it. A wall. It really feels like the repair work became real today. So, a question worth sitting with:

When did this entity actually become real?

Accounting-entity ≠ legal-entity
What You Worked Out
  • You wrote your own Operating Agreement — City Cycle’s (the one Anna set up) was only an example.
  • You signed it once for the Company and once as yourself — the two “you’s” made legal and literal.
  • The whole business was contributed in at book carryover — not one number changed; “what you put in” became members’ capital ($1,790 + $170 retained = $1,960).
  • Limited liability put a wall between the Company’s debts and your personal life — who can be pursued, not what the records show.
  • And the keystone: the accounting entity (since 1.1) is not the legal entity (days old). A legal body was wrapped around a boundary that already existed.
The deeper point

The same machine runs all the way up. A giant company draws one accounting boundary around dozens of separate legal companies; you drew one around an operation that, for now, has a single legal body younger than itself. The accounting line and the legal line are different lines, drawn for different reasons — and they rarely coincide.
