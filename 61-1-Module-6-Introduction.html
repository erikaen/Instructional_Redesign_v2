Module 6 Introduction

The turn

Same map, a different kind of entity

Module 5 took the map you built in the bike shop and held it up against a real, complex company — CVS Health. The map applied. CVS is owned by shareholders; its Reasons side carried the label shareholders’ equity; and the whole architecture — two balance sheets with three explanatory statements between them — held at the scale of a $250-billion company.

Module 6 holds the same map against a different kind of entity: Yale University. Yale is a nonprofit — a not-for-profit corporation that nobody owns. There are no shares, no shareholders, and no dividends. Hold that fact next to the map and a natural question follows: if the whole right side of the balance sheet was about owners and their claims, what happens to it when there are no owners?

The answer is the module. The map still applies — Assets minus Liabilities still computes to a difference, the difference still needs explaining, and the explaining statements still connect one balance sheet to the next. But the names change, the difference gets classified a different way, and the nonprofit world adds its own real complications. The map survives contact with an entity whose owners are absent — and the details are where the accounting lives.

What CVS confirmed

Six claims that survived contact with a real company

1
The identity holds at any scale.

On CVS’s balance sheets, recorded assets minus recorded liabilities equalled total shareholders’ equity exactly, at both dates: $253,538 − $178,156 = $75,382 (2025) and $253,215 − $177,485 = $75,730 (2024), in millions. A − L equals the recorded difference, on a company a hundred thousand times the size of the bike shop (53-1).

2
Cash gets its own explanation — read the labels.

CVS’s balance-sheet Cash line barely moved ($8,586 → $8,453, down $133M) against roughly $400 billion of revenue. The Cash Flow Statement explained the change — but only once you read the labels: the balance sheet reports “Cash and cash equivalents,” while the Cash Flow Statement reconciles a broader figure that includes restricted cash ($8,884 → $8,712, down $172M). Each statement explains a change defined by its own scope (53-2, 53-3).

3
Equity changes only through the statements.

CVS’s shareholders’ equity went down $348M ($75,730 → $75,382) in a year with positive net income — and the Statement of Shareholders’ Equity traced every mover: contributed +$769M, earned & retained −$1,115M, noncontrolling interests −$2M, summing to the −$348M (53-4). Equity cannot jump to a new balance; it must show the path.

4
The income statement explains the generated part.

Behind the single “Net income” mover of $1,768M = $1,728M + $40M ($1,728M to CVS shareholders plus $40M to noncontrolling interests) sat a $402-billion year of operations. Add other comprehensive income and comprehensive income reconciled: $1,768 + $526 = $2,294M attributable to CVS (53-5).

5
The whole architecture is one connected system.

Two balance sheets, three explanatory statements between them, every change accounted for (53-6).

6
Restrictive recognition still draws its line.

CVS’s own brand — decisive to its business — is nowhere on CVS’s balance sheet, off the books by the same convention that kept the bike shop’s owner-skill and customer base off its ledger. Recorded does not mean everything the entity has.

The map, in one line

A − L = a difference whose name depends on the entity; a series of balance sheets connected by statements that explain every change in it; and a recording convention that draws a restrictive line around what gets in. All six claims are about to hold again — for an entity with no owners at all.

What changes when nobody owns the place

New names, new classifications, same architecture

Everything that changes traces back to one fact: no owners. Walk it through in the order the map is built.

1
The identity is untouched.

Yale’s balance sheet still sets recorded assets against recorded liabilities, and the difference still falls out of the arithmetic. On Yale’s FY2025 statements: $59,043 − $9,672 = $49,371 (millions), and it held the year before too: $55,315 − $9,278 = $46,037. The difference is still definitional: recorded assets minus recorded liabilities — no more, no less.

2
The difference gets a new name, for a real reason.

The labels equities and shareholders’ equity arose in a for-profit context where ownership exists. Yale has no owners, so that naming convention does not fit. The difference is instead called Net Assets. The change is in the name, not in the nature of the thing: Net Assets is still that definitional difference, not a pool of cash and not anyone’s ownership claim.

3
Net Assets is classified by donor restriction, not by source.

In the bike shop and at CVS, the difference was split by where it came from — contributed by owners versus generated by operations. A nonprofit has no owner contributions, so that split does not apply. Instead, Net Assets is split into two categories that track the status of donor restrictions:

  • Net Assets Without Donor Restrictions — contributions that never carried a donor restriction, or that did and have since had the restriction satisfied.
  • Net Assets With Donor Restrictions — contributions that came in with a donor-imposed purpose or time restriction that has not yet been met.

At Yale, most of the difference is restricted: $38,609M with donor restrictions against $10,762M without — about 78% of net assets bound by donor intent.

4
Only donors create binding restrictions.

Yale’s board can designate resources for a purpose — including $7,734M it intends to treat as endowment — but a board designation is a statement of intent the board can change at any meeting. It is not a restriction, and it stays in Without Donor Restrictions. Only an outside donor can impose a binding constraint that lands a dollar in the With category.

5
The statements are renamed — and there are only two explaining ones, not three.

The balance sheet is called the Statement of Financial Position (SFP). The income statement’s job is done by the Statement of Activities, whose bottom line is change in net assets, not net income. It does the work of both CVS’s income statement and its statement of changes in equity, because for a nonprofit every change in net assets flows through this one statement. The cash story is still told by a Statement of Cash Flows. Yale’s architecture is two balance sheets with two explanatory statements between them, where CVS had three.

6
Restrictions release — a move with no for-profit equivalent.

When a donor’s purpose or time restriction is finally met, the dollars are released from restrictions and reclassified from With Donor Restrictions to Without. No new resources arrive; total net assets do not change; the amount simply moves from one category to the other. Yale reclassified $1,615M this way in FY2025.

7
The endowment is the elephant in the room.

Yale holds $49,708M of investments at fair value — the endowment — which dominates the $59-billion asset side. Its return, its spending allocation, and the contributions that feed it drive most of what the Statement of Activities reports. The endowment’s mechanics — a spending rule and a total-return concept — are a nonprofit-specific complication this course introduces at an awareness level, not for mastery.

8
A total is not a spending account.

Large Net Assets do not mean large spendable resources. Net Assets is the difference the accounting identity computes, not a pile of cash; the resources behind it can be tied up in buildings or locked inside a donor-restricted endowment. “How much can Yale actually spend soon?” is an asset-side, liquidity question, answered in a required footnote. Yale reports about $5,029M of financial assets available within one year, against $59 billion of total assets. Read the total for what it is: a classification of the difference, not a bank balance.

Where we are going

Yale University

Yale’s FY2025 financial report, for the year ended June 30, 2025, is the map made real for a nonprofit. The walk will show:

  • The Statement of Financial Position. $59,043M of recorded assets, $9,672M of recorded liabilities, and $49,371M of net assets — split $10,762M without donor restrictions and $38,609M with. Then comes a hard look at what the statement does NOT show: Yale’s collections — the Beinecke, the Art Gallery, and the Peabody specimens — sit at billions in fair value and zero on the books. They are not expensed and not recorded anywhere: a convention under ASC 958-360, not an oversight. The Yale name and its faculty are off the books for the same reason CVS’s brand is.
  • The cash question, again — with a nonprofit twist. Balance-sheet cash fell $293M ($1,058 → $765) while the Cash Flow Statement reports a $380M decrease — a label difference involving cash held inside the investment pool, reconciled in the footnotes. Same lesson as CVS’s restricted cash: read the labels.
  • The change-in-net-assets question. Net assets rose $3,334M in the year — but the Statement of Activities shows most of that increase is restricted through endowment return and donor gifts, and $1,615M of it is not new money at all but restrictions releasing. Was the change earned, given, or released? The bottom line is change in net assets, and its composition matters more than its size.
  • Two statements, not three. The Statement of Activities does the combined work of an income statement and a statement of changes in equity; the Statement of Cash Flows tells the cash story. Between the two Statements of Financial Position, they explain the whole year.
The promise of the module

These are examples, not the whole list — the endowment’s spending rule, the functional classification of expenses, and the collections election each carry further complication. The map survives contact with reality; the details are where the expertise lives.

About the name Net Assets

Why the nonprofit label fits

Module 5 warned you that the word equity promises more than the number can deliver: it is a definitional difference — assets minus liabilities — not a stake anyone could collect. A nonprofit makes the point unavoidable. Yale has no owners. There is no one whose stake this could be, no share to price, and no distribution that could ever be paid. So the profession does not call the difference “equity” here at all; it calls it Net Assets.

Do not let the plainer words fool you either. “Net Assets” can sound like “the assets, netted down” — a spendable amount. It is not. It is the same definitional difference: recorded assets minus recorded liabilities, whatever that difference happens to be. At Yale it is $49.4 billion — and most of it is either restricted by donors or tied up in the endowment and the buildings, not sitting in an account waiting to be spent.

What the name does buy you is a better fit to the accountability structure. A company answers to owners, so its difference is split by who put money in versus what operations generated. A nonprofit answers to donors and to its mission, so its difference is split by whether donors have restricted the money and whether those restrictions have been met. Same difference; different question asked of it. Read “Net Assets” the way Module 5 taught you to read “Equities” — as a classification of a computed difference, never as a pot of money with your name on it.
