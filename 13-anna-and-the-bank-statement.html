Tutorial 1.3: Anna and the Bank Statement

Bicycle repair shop · ~20 min

Progress0%
Dev: skip to phase

A few days after Maria the insurance agent came by, your friend Anna stops in. She kept the books at City Cycle for years before her kid, and she still does her own brake jobs.

Since Maria’s visit, you took the Insurable Property list you’d prepared for her and put all of it into your Excel Things list — every item priced, grouped by category. You can collapse a category to one line, or expand it to see everything inside. This is what you pull up to show Anna.

ANNA
“Nice — you actually put numbers on everything. That’s more than most people do.” She looks it over. “But this is all stuff — tools, parts, the laptop. Where’s your cash? None of this is money. You can’t tell how the shop’s doing without finding the cash first.”
What You’ll Discover
  • A bank statement is the bank’s record, mixed in with your personal life — you have to pull the repair work’s money out of it.
  • Cash going in and out isn’t the same as what you have and what you owe.
  • Along the way, a second list appears — the things you’ve promised.

You’ve been using your personal bank account for shop activity. Last month’s statement just arrived. Anna sits down at the workbench with you to look it over.

ANNA
“First thing: there’s a lot in here that has nothing to do with the shop. Apartment rent, groceries, your paycheck. Let’s pull out only the shop-related transactions so we can look at them clearly. Click info on any row to see what it was, then classify it as shop-related or personal.”
ANNA
“Good — that’s the shop’s cash, pulled out of your personal account. Twelve lines.” (It really would be simpler if the repair work just had its own bank account.)
ANNA
“$1,340.00 in, −$2,300.00 out. Net −$960.00 for May. So — does that −$960.00 mean the repair work went backwards this month?”
What do you think?
ANNA
“Right. Cash isn’t the whole story. Some of what went out bought you things you still have. Some of what came in came with strings attached. You can’t answer that from the cash alone — you have to go through it, line by line. Start with the money coming in.”

Anna stopped on the eight customer deposits — money coming in from customers across the month. The bank statement showed them as money in, nothing more. But what were they for?

ANNA
“So these deposits are from cash you got from customers, but why did they give it to you? Let’s check the receipts.”
Some receipts are clearer than others. For each deposit, decide what it was — or whether you can tell at all.

You dig out last month’s credit-card statement. Same story as the checking account — the shop’s charges are tangled up with your own.

ANNA
“Same drill as the bank. Go down the card and mark each charge: is it the repair work, or personal? Tap info on a row to see what it was.”

Two promises now — Smith’s frame and the card. You add another sheet to the workbook, right next to Things and Cash, and call it Promises. One workbook: what you have, what you owe, and the cash you pulled.

ANNA
“There you go. Keep them side by side. Click between the tabs whenever you want to see one or the other.”

Both promises are down on the list. Anna turns back to the bank statement and runs her finger up to the biggest shop line of the month — the very first one.

May 1 · Check #1041
rent
−$1,300.00
ANNA
“$1,300 for rent is a lot for one month around here. The bank statement only tells us it was paid to Westville Properties — it doesn’t say what for. Got the lease handy?”

Late afternoon. You and Anna have been at it for hours, and your workbook has a few tabs now — what you have, what you owe, and the cash you pulled. Time to give the have-and-owe lists their proper names.

Tutorial Complete!
What You Discovered
  • The bank statement is the bank’s record, mixed in with your personal life. You had to pull the repair work’s cash out of it — net −$960 for the month.
  • Not all money in is yours to keep: Smith’s $220 was an advance — cash you have and a promise you owe. It started a second list: Promises.
  • A promise only goes on the list when something happens to create it: Jones asked for a frame too, but put nothing down — so it stayed off.
  • The credit card is a promise to pay — you sorted it just like the bank ($2,030 repair charges − $800 paid down = $1,230 owed).
  • Not all money out is gone: half the rent bought a future month — a prepaid asset you still hold.
  • Your two lists earned their names: Assets ($3,410) and Liabilities ($1,450).
The deeper point

A list of cash going in and out isn’t the same as what you have and owe. You had to interpret the bank’s record — separate the repair from the personal, tell an advance from money earned, split a rent payment into used-up and paid-ahead — to see your real picture. That interpretation is what turns a bank statement into a set of Assets and Liabilities. The records and the economic reality are not the same thing.
