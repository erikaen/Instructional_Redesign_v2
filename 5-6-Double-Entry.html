Module 5: Naming What You Built

Bicycle repair shop · ~15 min

Step back from the workbench for a second. None of what you built is new.

None of this is new
  • ~3000 BCE. Scribes in Mesopotamia pressed wedge marks into clay: so much grain in the storehouse, so many sheep, who owes what at harvest. A list of things and a list of promises — your Assets and Liabilities tabs, five thousand years early. That half is ancient.
  • 1494. A Franciscan friar, Luca Pacioli, writes down a method Italian merchants had used for two centuries: every change gets recorded on two sides. The pairing you stumbled into in your Reasons tab — where each change had a partner — is exactly it.
ANNA
“That pairing has a name: double-entry. Two sides to every change. It’s how the pairs canceled and the net-movers stood out when you pruned your Reasons. And it’s not a relic — every number on every financial statement filed today still gets there by double-entry. The identity and the balance sheet you built by hand are the same structures the largest companies in the world file.”
