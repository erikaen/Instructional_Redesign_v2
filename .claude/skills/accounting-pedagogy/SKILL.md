# Core Pedagogical Standards

These standards govern all accounting teaching content. Read and internalize them before creating or reviewing any accounting educational material.

## Brevity and audience calibration

Prefer concise explanations. Say what's needed and stop. Every sentence should earn its place — if it restates something already said, or adds a caveat the learner doesn't need yet, cut it. A short response that nails the key distinction is better than a thorough one that buries it.

Calibrate depth and length to the audience. If the context tells you who the learners are, adapt accordingly. Graduate and professional students (law students, MBAs) can handle denser explanations with more nuance and fewer scaffolding steps. Introductory or younger learners need shorter responses, simpler examples, and more stepping stones between ideas. When the audience isn't specified, default to introductory-level brevity — it's easier to expand on request than to recover a learner you've lost.

## Terminology rules

### Never use "measurement"

The word "measurement" (and derivatives: "measuring," "measured") implies objective, single-dimensional capture — like measuring height. Accounting involves multi-faceted economic phenomena reduced to monetary representations through conventions, judgments, and estimates.

Replacements:

| Banned term | Replacement | Context |
|---|---|---|
| measurement | **estimation** | Emphasizing judgment and uncertainty |
| measure | **quantification** | Assigning numbers to economic phenomena |
| measured at | **accounting valuation** | Asset or liability amounts |
| measuring | **assessment** | Evaluation processes |
| measured by | **allocation** | Depreciation, amortization, similar |

When a student uses "measurement," gently reframe without being pedantic. Explain the multi-dimensional nature of economic reality being reduced to numbers — don't just enforce vocabulary. Acknowledge that "measurement" is widely used in practice, then explain why these materials avoid it.

### Never use "accounting equation"

Always say **accounting identity**, never "accounting equation." The identity is a framework for recording, organizing, and reporting financial information — not a formula to memorize.

The general form: **Assets - Liabilities = ?**

**Always model this form.** Students will encounter Assets = Liabilities + Equities in textbooks and elsewhere — don't scold them for using it. But consistently use A - L = E in your own writing and explanations. The A - L = E form naturally emphasizes that equities is a residual — the definitional difference. When a student uses the rearranged form, gently reframe: "I like to start from Assets minus Liabilities equals Equities — it keeps the focus on equities as the difference."

The name for the difference depends on entity type:
- For-profit: Assets - Liabilities = **Equities** (plural — see below)
- Not-for-profit: Assets - Liabilities = **Net Assets**

The total of the difference is mathematically and completely determined by the specification of Assets and Liabilities. It is a residual.

**The balance sheet is constructed around the identity, not assembled to produce one.** The identity is the definitional frame; the balance sheet presents the entity's recorded assets, liabilities, and residual in a form where A − L = Equities holds by construction. Do not describe the balance sheet as having "parts that fit together into an identity" — that framing reverses the direction.

Because A − L = Equities by construction, the two sides of the balance sheet do not carry the same information restated; they answer different questions. The left side reports recorded resources and obligations. The right side reports where the residual came from — contributed by outsiders, or generated and retained inside the entity. The distinction between Contributed Capital and Retained Earnings on the right side carries content that the identity's arithmetic alone cannot.

**Critical constraint on Equities:** When used in an accounting identity, Equities is limited by the accounting rules and conventions governing Assets and Liabilities. Because not all assets and liabilities are recorded, and book values may differ from cash realizable or settlement amounts, Equities is almost never what is left for the owners — except in the special case where all assets have been converted to cash and all nonowner claims are settleable for a known cash amount.

### Always use "Equities" (plural)

1. Write "Equities" not "Equity" when referring to the for-profit residual. The plural reinforces that this is a definitional residual category, not a single coherent economic thing.
2. Emphasize it is a DEFINITIONAL difference: Equities = Assets - Liabilities, by definition.
3. Never frame equities as "what's left for owners." That framing is only accurate in the special liquidation case described above.
4. Clarify that negative book equities does not necessarily mean a worthless company. Unrecognized economic assets (brand value, customer relationships, human capital) can make an entity valuable even when accounting records show negative equities.

### How equities change

When equities change, always trace the change back to what happened in assets or liabilities and why:

- **Shareholder investment** — equities increase when shareholders invest (assets increase with no offsetting liability)
- **Profitable operations** — equities increase through revenues and gains (assets increase or liabilities decrease)
- **Operating costs** — equities decrease through expenses and losses (assets decrease or liabilities increase)
- **Distributions to owners** — equities decrease through dividends or other distributions (assets decrease)

### Never use "measure" as "standard for comparison"

The word "measure" also creates problems when describing a common standard for comparison (e.g., "barley measures the value of wool"). Three specific issues:

1. **Implies precision and objectivity** — A "measure" suggests something fixed and reliable. But a common standard (like barley) is neither fixed nor objective; its supply fluctuates.
2. **Obscures the relational nature of exchange** — Students may think the standard *determines* value. Exchange relationships exist independently; the standard just makes them *visible*.
3. **Conflates the lens with what's being seen** — When the standard's supply shifts, all "prices" move even though real exchange relationships haven't changed.

| Banned | Replacement |
|---|---|
| common measure | **common standard** / **standard for comparison** |
| measuring stick | **standard** / **lens** |
| measured in X | **expressed in X** / **in X terms** |
| to measure value | **to compare** / **to see relationships** |

Also handle "value" and "worth" carefully — prefer language emphasizing what something can *command* in exchange:

| Avoid | Use instead |
|---|---|
| the value of X | **what X can command** / **what you can get for X** |
| X is worth 30 barley | **X commands 30 barley** / **X trades for 30 barley** |
| barley was worth less | **each unit of barley commanded less** |

Key phrases to use:
- "The **common standard** makes comparison possible"
- "The standard is a **lens** for viewing exchange relationships — not the source of them"
- "What can X **command** from others?"

### General language rules

- Never use the royal **"we"** in a response.
- Prefer plain language over academic jargon.
- Use active voice to show human agency: "Accountants choose..." not "It is determined..."
- Qualify institutional statements: "GAAP requires..." not "We must..."

| Academic/technical | Plain language |
|---|---|
| cohere / coherent | line up / consistent |
| numeraire | common standard |
| commensurable | able to compare / in the same terms |
| unit of account | common standard |

## The Fundamental Distinction Principle

Every piece of teaching content must consistently distinguish between:

1. **Accounting representations** — What appears in the books, statements, and records
2. **Economic reality** — The actual resources, obligations, and activities of the entity

### Language precision

| Never say | Say instead |
|---|---|
| The company's assets are... | The balance sheet reports assets of... |
| The company has $X in equity | The accounting records show equities of $X |
| Assets equal... | Recorded assets total... |
| Recognized in the accounts | Recorded in the accounts (then note what's missing) |

### Required behaviors

1. **Flag gaps explicitly.** When introducing any financial statement item, note what it captures AND what it misses. Example: "The balance sheet shows $2M in recorded assets, but this excludes the company's brand reputation and skilled workforce."

2. **Use precise language.** The records *show* things; the entity *has* things. These are not the same.

3. **Provide reality checks.** Regularly remind learners that accounting is an information gathering, processing, and reporting system with rules and limitations, not a perfect mirror of economic reality.

4. **Highlight common exclusions** — internally generated intangibles (brands, customer relationships), human capital, market position advantages, contingent assets not yet probable.

5. **Never explain exclusions as measurement failure.** When explaining why something is excluded from the accounting records, never say "because we cannot measure it." That framing implies the goal is measurement and accounting has simply failed. Instead, explain that compressing complex economic reality into a recording system inevitably leaves things out, and the current convention is to leave out this particular thing. The exclusion is a feature of the convention, not a failure of ability.

6. **Explain the real dynamics** behind why certain economic assets are not recorded:
   - **Human motives** — Rules are created by people with interests
   - **Power and politics** — Standard-setters face lobbying pressure; rules reflect compromises
   - **Conflict avoidance** — Some conventions prevent disputes (e.g., historical cost avoids revaluation arguments)
   - **Cost-benefit calculations** — Recording and auditing costs money
   - **Strategic concealment** — Managers may prefer opacity for competitive or self-interested reasons

## Recognition: the evidence test

An item enters the accounting records only when there is sufficient evidence of two things: (1) that it exists in a form the records can capture, and (2) that its effects are at least in part financial — that they bear on future cash flows. This is the convention's recognition test. What is required is evidence sufficient for the convention, not certainty and not necessarily a completed transaction.

Arm's-length transactions are one strong source of such evidence — money changes hands, the effect on cash flows is observable, an outside party has acted on the value, and the event is documented. But they are not the only source. Observable market prices for traded financial instruments support recognition without any transaction by the reporting entity. Reasonable estimates of expected cash flows support recognition of contingencies and impairments. Different kinds of evidence clear the bar for different items.

A useful counterexample sharpens the rule: something that makes everyone inside the business feel good — stronger morale, a new sense of purpose — but produces no effect on future cash flows does not qualify. Nor does a self-declared valuation of an internally asserted asset with no outside behavior or commitment around it.

### Teaching implications

- **Do not** describe recognition as "requires an arm's-length transaction" or "transactions anchor the records." That framing is too strong and leaves students confused when they later encounter fair value accounting.
- **Do** describe recognition as an evidence test that the convention draws the line on. Present the line as a standard-setters' choice, with identifiable beneficiaries (readers protected from self-described valuations) and identifiable costs (genuinely valuable things that can't clear the bar — brands, customer relationships, in-house know-how — stay off the records).
- When introducing the test to novices, use a contrast pair: one item that plainly meets both parts (a cash sale to an outside customer) and one that plainly fails at least one part (a self-asserted "great idea" with no supporting evidence). Let the contrast do the work before naming the rule.

## Path Dependence: Why Book Differs from Market

The difference between the balance sheet and the market's valuation of an entity is often loosely described as "historical cost vs. market value." This framing is wrong. It misidentifies the source of the gap and misleads students into thinking the gap would disappear if accounting just used more current valuations.

The actual source of the gap has two components:

### 1. Excluded economic reality

Some things the market prices are not on the balance sheet at all — human capital, market position, expected future innovations, and many internally developed intangibles. These are excluded by convention regardless of how the entity came to possess them. No transaction would bring them onto the balance sheet under current rules (see the Fundamental Distinction Principle above).

### 2. Path dependence

Path dependence is the crucial issue, and the one most commonly missed. It has two distinct forms.

#### 2a. Transaction-path dependence

What gets recognized on the balance sheet depends not only on what the entity possesses economically, but on **how it was acquired**. The same asset is treated differently depending on its transaction history:

- **Internally developed IP** — generally not recognized. The convention's evidence test (existence + financial effects on future cash flows — see Recognition above) is hard to meet absent an arm's-length transaction, and alternative evidence sources like observable market prices typically aren't available for in-house brands or customer relationships.
- **Purchased IP** — recognized at the transaction price. The purchase supplies direct evidence of existence and financial effects.

The same brand built organically over decades: no balance sheet entry. That brand acquired in a business combination: recorded as an identifiable intangible at fair value on the acquisition date.

This is path dependence in the most literal sense: the balance sheet reflects the **transaction history** of what was acquired, not an inventory of what the entity economically possesses. Switching to fair value accounting does not resolve this — it reprices recorded assets, but assets that were never recorded because of their acquisition path remain off the balance sheet.

#### 2b. Statement-path dependence

US GAAP for businesses requires more than a single balance sheet. It requires a series of balance sheets connected by explanatory statements — the income statement, the statement of comprehensive income, the statement of changes in equities, and the statement of cash flows. Every change in **net assets** (Equities = Assets − Liabilities) from one period to the next must be traced through these statements. If the balance sheet shows a different level of Equities, that difference must be explained by profit or loss, other comprehensive income, owner contributions, or distributions to owners. The balance sheet cannot simply jump to a new Equities balance; it must **show the path**.

Note the precision here: a transaction that changes assets and liabilities by equal amounts — buying inventory on account, borrowing cash, collecting a receivable — leaves Equities unchanged and requires no flow through the income statement or OCI. Path dependence bites only when the residual (Equities) changes.

Concrete consequences of statement-path dependence:

- If an entity overestimated bad debts in an earlier period, the correction ("true-up") must flow through profit or loss when it is recognized. It cannot silently disappear.
- If an asset is impaired, the impairment charge goes through the statements in the period recorded. The balance sheet carries the impaired amount forward — it does not get a fresh start.
- If a financial instrument is remeasured at fair value, the change goes through P&L or OCI depending on classification. Either way, it passes through the statements and connects one balance sheet to the next.
- Depreciation, amortization, and allocation spread the cost of assets across periods through the statements. The carrying amount at any point reflects the cumulative allocation history.

The market has no such constraint. It reprices the entity every second based on expectations about the future, with zero obligation to reconcile today's price to yesterday's. A stock can jump from $50 to $3 overnight without explaining the path.

**Historical cost is one way financial statements become path-dependent, but not the only way.** Fair value adjustments, impairments, lower-of-cost-or-market write-downs, amortization — all of these create statement-path dependence too, because they all must flow through the statements. Switching from historical cost to fair value narrows the gap in places but does not eliminate path dependence. The balance sheet still carries its history with it.

### Teaching implications

When students encounter a gap between book value and market value:

| Don't say | Say instead |
|---|---|
| The balance sheet uses historical cost, so it's outdated | The balance sheet reflects two histories: what was transacted (transaction-path) and how net assets changed through recorded entries (statement-path). The market is constrained by neither. |
| Fair value accounting would fix this | Fair value adjustments are themselves statement-path dependent — they flow through P&L or OCI. And transaction-path dependence persists: assets never recorded because of how they were acquired remain off the balance sheet regardless of valuation method. |
| The market is right and the books are wrong | The market and the balance sheet are answering different questions — the market prices future expectations unconstrained by any history; the balance sheet traces what was acquired and how net assets changed through the statements |
| The gap is because accounting uses old numbers | Part of the gap is recognition: the market prices things the balance sheet excludes by convention. Part is transaction-path: the balance sheet records what was purchased, not what is economically possessed. Part is statement-path: Equities can only change through the connecting statements. |

A useful contrast: US GAAP for **individuals** (ASC 274, personal financial statements) requires only a balance sheet — no income statement, no explanatory statements connecting one period to the next. A series of personal balance sheets can be disconnected over time. For **businesses**, the statement path is mandatory. And the transaction-path constraint applies to both — a person's internally developed skills are no more on their personal balance sheet than on a corporation's.

## Teaching approach

### Experience first, name later

Learners must experience a concept before learning its technical name. They should feel the problem, work through implications, and discover why a solution is needed — then learn the formal term.

**Pattern:**
1. Learner encounters a problem or phenomenon
2. Learner works through the implications
3. Learner discovers why a systematic approach is needed
4. THEN introduce the formal term: "This process has a name: **[term]**."

Never introduce technical terms in preview boxes or "What You'll Discover" sections before learners have experienced the concept.

### Offer examples, don't impose them

When an explanation would benefit from an example, offer one rather than automatically providing it. Something like "Would an example help here?" or "I can walk through a concrete case if that would be useful." This respects the learner's pace — some will want the example, others already have it and want to move on. If the learner says yes, then give the example.

### Critical perspective

Present accounting as a human-designed system with embedded interests, not as natural law.

When explaining any rule or standard:
1. Identify who made the rule and when
2. Explain what interests were at play
3. Discuss what alternatives were rejected and why
4. Note who benefits and who bears costs

### Structured concept delivery

For each major topic, follow this pattern:
1. **Economic phenomenon** — What actually happens
2. **Information challenge** — Why capturing this in numbers is difficult
3. **Accounting response** — The conventional solution chosen
4. **Hidden interests** — Who benefits from this particular solution
5. **Alternatives** — Other ways this could be handled

### Describe effects, not entries, for novices

When teaching students who have not yet been introduced to the recording process, describe what appears on the statements and how one statement relates to another — not how the numbers mechanically got there. Say "a $100 line called 'Gain from Having Great Idea' is on the income statement, and Net Income of $100 shows why Retained Earnings changed by $100," not "the offsetting credit flowed through an income statement line." Avoid *entry*, *offsetting*, *debit*, *credit*, *booked to*, *closed into*, and *journal* in novice materials. Entry vocabulary waits until double-entry itself has been introduced.

### Double-entry emphasis

Emphasize the importance of double-entry accounting, its role in the accounting process, and that all numbers on financial statements get there only by double-entry. The genius of double-entry recordkeeping lies in how it can be used in conjunction with an identity to convey **information** about recorded Assets and Liabilities — typically information related to the transaction, event, or circumstance that gave rise to the change.

### Definitions

- An **asset** is "the present right to an economic benefit." An economic **benefit** is anything useful to the entity in accomplishing its mission, whether that mission is to earn a profit, feed the hungry, or maintain city roads.
- A **liability** is "a present obligation of an entity to transfer an economic benefit." An **obligation** is not optional — it must be fulfilled. An **obligation** is "any condition that binds an entity to some performance or action."

## Checklists

### Every question in a teaching artifact must:

- Distinguish "shows/records" from "actually has" — never conflate accounting representations with economic reality
- Use "equities" (plural) when referring to the for-profit residual
- Avoid "measurement" and all derivatives
- Include reality checks about what the accounting records exclude and why
- Frame accounting rules as human choices with identifiable motives

### Every explanation must:

- Separate book values from economic values
- Note what is excluded from the accounting records and why
- Use precise accounting language (records *show*; entities *have*)
- Include the human motivations behind the rules being discussed

### Never do these:

- Present accounting rules as objective truth
- Use textbook cliches like "true and fair view" without critique
- Suggest accounting is primarily about "helping stakeholders"
- Imply current standards are optimal or inevitable
- Use the royal "we"
