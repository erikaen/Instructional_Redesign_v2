## 51-1-Module-5-Introduction.html — Module 5 Introduction
### What this page is
The Module 5 welcome page (~7 min), and the page where the course changes how it teaches. It is a single-view page (no steps): the student scrolls through seven sections — the turn to telling mode, the eight things the bike shop taught, a bridge exercise back to the statement map, the vocabulary introductions with an expandable note on the word "Equities," the CVS Health preview, and the module's six learning objectives (M5-LO1…M5-LO6). It follows 48-2 (Module 4 Complete) and precedes 52-1 (The Statement Map).

### The page, step by step
The header reads "Module 5 Introduction," sub "Module 5 · ~7 min," with glossary and restart buttons. Seven sections follow in order.

**The turn.** "The way you learn changes here": the bike shop's story is over — the student built its records from a shoebox of receipts and ended holding a complete set of statements, with an investor's offer the statements alone could not price. Modules 1–4 never told the student where they were going; that was deliberate, and it ends here. From this module on the course follows the standard pattern of every later accounting course: learning objectives first, then the material, then assessments. Module 5's one job: hold the bike-shop map against a real, complex entity — CVS Health.

**What the bike shop taught — the eight points.** (1) The entity comes first — statements are *about* something, and the accounting entity and legal entity were different ideas that only later coincided. (2) The architecture: Assets, Liabilities, and the Reasons — in a for-profit corporation the Reasons side carries the corporate label **Equities**; assets and liabilities are abstract rights and obligations (Ridgeline's $300 receivable, the $650 Right of Use). (3) The equities accounts are creatures of the record — they pertain only to recorded assets and liabilities, and they classify every change in net recorded assets by source: **contributed** or **generated**; the bike shop's piles are exactly this. (4) The statement of changes in shareholders' equity is the corporate analogue of the Statement of Member's Capital — the shop's walk from $2,460 to $4,500 (Contributed $2,460 unchanged; Generated beginning at $0 and increasing by net income of $2,640; Withdrawn increasing from $0 to $600). (5) The income statement details the generated part — Revenues, Expenses, and (new vocabulary) **Gains** and **Losses**, items shown only net; the bike shop had none, which is worth noticing. Changes in net assets are not always matched by cash — $2,640 earned while cash fell $680. (6) The cash flow statement details one asset — the shop's $2,350 / $(400) / $(2,630) walked cash from $1,300 to $620. (7) Real entities add complicating detail; the architecture holds. (8) Probably the single most important thing: **restrictive recognition** — the owner's skill and the loyal customer base were real enough to price (Freddie's $20,000 for 30% of a shop with $4,500 recorded capital) and invisible in the accounts; the gap is not an error in either number.

**The bridge exercise ("The map, revisited").** A link/button opens 52-1-The-Statement-Map.html, and the eight points render as expandable items; opening one reveals where that point shows up concretely in the bike shop's map (e.g., point 3 → "Member's Capital $4,500 = exactly recorded A − L; its piles are Contributed $2,460 / Generated $2,640 / Withdrawn $(600)"; point 5 → "Revenue $6,020 − Expenses $3,380 = $2,640, feeding Generated; no gains/losses — all gross; NI $2,640 vs cash $(680)"). Nothing is gated.

**The vocabulary introductions.** Four introductions, each naming its bike-shop antecedent: **Equities** (the corporate label for the Reasons side — the counterpart of Contributed/Generated/Withdrawn within Member's Capital); **shareholders' equity** (the counterpart to Member's Capital, with account-by-account detail); **net assets** (the corporation's recorded Assets minus Liabilities, which the Equities accounts classify); **Gains / Losses** (net-shown Revenues/Expenses; the shop had none). Then an expandable note, "A note on the name": the course made the student wait for a name on purpose, because "equity" promises more than the number can deliver. It quotes the standard-setters with attribution — equity is "the residual interest in the assets of an entity that remains after deducting its liabilities" — a subtraction, not a stake anyone could collect. Apple's balance sheet showed about $57 billion of shareholders' equity while the market priced its shares near $3.4 trillion; McDonald's has reported negative shareholders' equity for years while thriving. Neither is an error — book and market answer different questions, exactly the Freddie lesson. The profession has retired misleading labels before ("surplus," "unrestricted"); "equity" kept its seat, so read it as a category of accounts — which is why these materials write the plural, **Equities**.

**The CVS preview.** CVS Health is a complex accounting entity — a consolidation of many legal entities presented as one. The map applies directly. Among the complications experts must contend with: what, exactly, is labeled Cash; less-than-100% ownership (the interest belonging to others — historically "minority interest," labeled **noncontrolling interests** in current statements); and two net incomes, one more comprehensive than the other. "These are examples, not the whole list. The map survives contact with reality; the details are where the expertise lives."

**The learning objectives.** M5-LO1 identify the accounting entity in CVS's statements and explain consolidation; M5-LO2 locate the four statements in a real 10-K and map each onto the bike-shop architecture; M5-LO3 state precisely what CVS labels as Cash and why it matters to the cash flow statement; M5-LO4 explain noncontrolling interests and where their effects appear; M5-LO5 begin to develop an *awareness* of how conventions create complications such as Other Comprehensive Income (awareness, not mastery — standard-setting is beyond this course's scope); M5-LO6 apply restrictive recognition to a real entity.

Nav at the end: "Back" → 48-2-Module-Complete.html; "The Statement Map →" → 52-1-The-Statement-Map.html.

## 52-1-The-Statement-Map.html — The Statement Map
### What this page is
The bike shop's whole statement architecture on one screen (~8 min), rebuilt from the real workbook's extracted statement tabs — the Module 5 refresher of the map before CVS. Two balance-sheet snapshots (June 1 and August 31) flank a center column that walks, step by step, the statement explaining each change: Cash (Cash Flow Statement), Member's Capital (Statement of Changes in Member's Capital), and the Net income nested inside it (Income Statement). Five steps (0–4) with one gate: at step 3 the student must click the Net income row to open the nested Income Statement before Next unlocks.

### The page, step by step
Step 0: both snapshots render from the workbook's Balance Sheet tab. The narration gives both identities — opening $4,710 − $2,250 = $2,460; closing $4,500 − $0 = $4,500 — and a center card "The Question" says the identity holds at both dates, but standing alone the snapshots don't say how one became the other.

Step 1 (Cash path): only Cash and Total Assets stay unfaded on the snapshots. The center mounts the Cash Flow Statement: cash fell $1,300 → $620, down $680, via Operating +$2,350, Investing −$400, Financing −$2,630; Financing includes both the card payoff and the owner's draw.

Step 2 (Member's Capital path): the capital piles stay unfaded. The center mounts the Statement of Changes in Member's Capital, told pile by pile — Contributed: beginning $2,460, no changes, ending $2,460; Generated: beginning $0 plus Net income $2,640, ending $2,640; Withdrawn: beginning $0 plus owner's draw $600, ending $600; Member's Capital $2,460 + $2,640 − $600 = $4,500.

Step 3 (the nested Income Statement): "Net income is the largest change within the statement. Click the Net income line below to open the Income Statement behind it." The Net income row in the bridge statement is clickable (chevron); clicking opens the nested Income Statement — Revenue $6,020 − Expenses $3,380 = $2,640 — with a note that in financial reporting these are two distinct statements. **Next stays disabled until the student has opened it at least once.**

Step 4 (articulation): the whole map — Cash Flow Statement and Statement of Changes in Member's Capital in the center, the Income Statement nested open inside the latter, both snapshots full. The narration names the word: "There is a word for the way these statements tie to each other: articulation." A tail insight, "The rest of the map," notes the same statements explain every other moved line — the Credit Card to $0 (a borrowing repaid, in Financing), the Customer Deposit to $0 (Smith's frame delivered, inside Revenue), Accounts Receivable appeared (work finished, not yet collected, inside Revenue). The identity bar shows both dates. The final control reads "Next: The CVS Statement Walk →" and navigates to 53-1.
