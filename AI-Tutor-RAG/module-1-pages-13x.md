# RAG knowledge base — pages 13-1 through 13-9

This file documents nine consecutive pages of the interactive accounting prework, in the order a student meets them. It is written for the AI Tutor that helps students who are lost, confused, or unsure of the directions. Every “how to help” entry below follows the course’s discovery style: the tutor orients the student first, then offers progressively stronger hints, and only confirms an answer after genuine attempts — mirroring the pages’ own three-strike (hint, stronger hint, reveal) spirit.

Conduct reminders baked into every section below: the course teaches by discovery, so the tutor never announces what a page is about to reveal. The repair work is an activity, not an actor — it cannot own, owe, or agree; documents and time periods are not actors either. Records SHOW things; entities HAVE things. The tutor mirrors the course’s language: “accounting identity,” never the phrase that names the balancing expression. The tutor never names the Assets-minus-Liabilities difference in this material — here it is only “the difference.” The tutor never states that personal money quietly covered repair-work costs (a discovery the student reaches on their own at the negative cash tally). The tutor never says “Member’s Capital,” never does useful-life math, and never reveals that revenue and expenses are a sorting of generated rows. A hidden triple-click dev mode exists but is instructor-only; the tutor never points a student to it.

## 13-1-Meet-Anna.html — Module 3 — Meet Anna
### What this page is
This is the opening page of the module. Anna, a friend who kept the books at City Cycle and still does her own brake jobs, stops in a few days after the insurance agent Maria visited. The student sees the Things list (the Insurable Property list from Maria’s visit, now entered into the Excel workbook with dollar values and collapsible categories) and Anna explains that the list is only part of the picture — the cash moving through the bank account is still missing. It sets up the whole module: separating repair activity from personal activity, and learning that some payments leave a promise behind and some leave a thing behind. Nothing on this page is interactive — it is narrative plus a read-only workbook view, ended by a Continue.

### The page, step by step
1. A narrative block explains that since Maria’s visit the student entered the Insurable Property list into an Excel workbook as a Things list, each item with a dollar value, grouped by collapsible category. That is what the student pulls up to show Anna.
2. The Excel workbook renders (Bike-Repair.xlsx, Things sheet). Categories shown: EQUIPMENT, COMPUTER EQUIPMENT, SUPPLIES, FIXTURES, each with a subtotal, and a TOTAL row. The student can click the +/− toggle on a category to expand it and see the items inside, but nothing has to be done — it is a read-only view.
3. Anna’s dialog box appears: she praises the dollar values but says the list is still only part of the picture and asks where the cash is — they need to look at money moving through the bank account.
4. A “What You’ll Discover” card lists three upcoming discoveries (the bank statement mixes repair work with personal activity; cash moving in/out is not the same as what’s tied up in the repair work and what’s still owed; some payments leave a promise behind, some leave a thing behind).
5. The page ends with the standard “You’ve finished this section — click Next -> to continue.” message. Clicking Next advances. There is no task to complete on this page, so Continue is effectively the Next control.

### If the student is lost
- “I don’t know what I’m supposed to do here.” → Orient: this page has no task; it just introduces Anna and shows the Things list. Ask what Anna says is still missing from the picture (the cash). Nudge: point them to the “What You’ll Discover” card — those three bullets preview the module. Stronger hint: tell them nothing is unlocked by doing; just read the narrative and Anna’s dialog, then click Next.
- “What is the Things list?” → Orient: ask where this list came from. Nudge: it is the Insurable Property list prepared for Maria, now in Excel with dollar values. Stronger hint: have them click a + toggle to expand a category and see the priced items inside.
- “Why does Anna say the list is only part of the picture?” → Orient: ask what the list shows versus what it does not show. Nudge: the list shows things tied up in the repair work; it does not show money moving. Stronger hint: Anna says they need to look at the money moving through the bank account — that is the next page.

### TUTOR-ONLY answer key
TUTOR-ONLY: There is no graded answer on this page. The Things list totals: EQUIPMENT subtotal, COMPUTER EQUIPMENT $610 (Laptop), SUPPLIES subtotal, FIXTURES subtotal, with a grand TOTAL. The key takeaway the tutor may confirm after the student articulates it: the Things list shows what’s tied up in the repair work, but it does not show cash moving or anything still owed — those come next. The tutor should not announce the upcoming discoveries before the student reads them; let the “What You’ll Discover” card do the revealing.

### Anchor
anchor: The bank statement mixes the repair work with personal activity, so you have to separate them first.

### Before and after
Before: the student finished the prior material (the Insurable Property list for Maria). After: the student moves to sorting the bank statement into repair-related versus personal rows.

## 13-2-The-Bank-Statement.html — Module 3 — Sort the Bank Statement
### What this page is
Anna sits down with the student to look over last month’s personal bank statement, which mixes repair-work activity with personal activity. The student’s job is to classify each of 18 rows as Repair or Personal by opening the row’s info, then choosing a category. A sort-progress panel tracks counts and unlocks a “Sort the rest” shortcut after enough correct placements; finally a “Check my sort” button verifies everything. This is the first sorting task in the module and teaches the recurring pattern: separate repair work from personal life before anything else.

### The page, step by step
1. A narrative explains the student has been using a personal bank account for the repair work; the May statement just arrived and Anna looks it over.
2. Anna’s dialog: clean it up first — there’s a lot with nothing to do with the repair work (paycheck, apartment rent, groceries). For each row decide whether it belongs with the repair work or personal life. Click the info (i) on any row to see what it was.
3. A sort-progress panel appears (“Sort the transactions”) with three count boxes — Repair-related, Personal, Unsorted — and a note that a shortcut to auto-sort the rest unlocks as you go; you’re welcome to sort all 18 yourself.
4. The bank statement renders (Elm City Bank, Personal Checking ending 8814, beginning balance $2,500.00, ending $4,074.00, May 1–May 31 2026). Each row has Date, Description, Withdrawal, Deposit, Balance, and an info button. Clicking the info button expands the row to show what it was and a Classify row with two buttons: Repair and Personal. Clicking a classify button assigns the category and collapses the row; a colored tag (Repair/Personal) appears on the row.
5. After the student has at least 5 correct placements including at least one Repair and one Personal, with rows still unsorted, the “Sort the rest” button becomes enabled (it auto-classifies all remaining rows correctly). The student may also keep sorting manually.
6. When Unsorted reaches 0, a “Check my sort” button appears. Clicking it marks any wrong rows with a “Try Again” tag and a red stripe; Anna says not all look right yet — try again. Wrong rows can be re-opened and re-classified. When all are correct, each correct row shows a green check, and a Reset button appears.
7. When all rows are correct, Anna confirms: now we can see which rows belong with the repair work and which with personal life. Next, pull the repair-related rows together and see how cash moved during May. The Next -> message unlocks.

### If the student is lost

- “The Check my sort button says some are wrong.” → Orient: wrong rows have a “Try Again” tag and red stripe. Nudge: re-open those rows and re-classify. Stronger hint: a common mix-up is the May 1 rent check (repair — it’s the rented workspace) versus the May 5 apartment rent autopay (personal).
- “Do I have to sort all 18?” → Orient: no. Nudge: after enough correct placements the “Sort the rest” button unlocks and auto-sorts the remainder. Stronger hint: you can keep sorting manually if you prefer — both paths are fine.

## 13-3-The-Cash-Movement.html — Module 3 — The Cash Movement
### What this page is
Anna presents the Cash sheet — the 10 repair-related rows pulled from the bank statement, separated into Cash in and Cash out, totaling a net cash flow of −$160 for May. The student then answers a multiple-choice question about whether −$160 alone tells whether May was a good or bad month. The page teaches that a cash-flow number by itself is not the whole story. This is the first place the negative cash tally appears, and the tutor must not announce what it implies about personal money covering costs — the student reasons toward that themselves.

### The page, step by step
1. Anna’s dialog: the 10 repair-related rows were pulled out of the bank statement; it would be simpler if there were a separate bank account just for the repair work, but for now this is a cleaner view. In May $1,340 came in and $1,500 went out, a net cash flow of −$160.
2. The Excel workbook shows with two tabs: Things and Cash (Cash active). The Cash sheet lists “Bike-repair cash flows — May 1 – May 31, 2026,” with collapsible Cash in and Cash out groups and a NET CASH FLOW (May) row of −$160. A footnote says it is pulled from the repair-related transactions, the month’s flow (cash in minus cash out), not a balance. The student can expand/collapse groups but there is no entry task here.
3. A multiple-choice card asks: the Cash sheet shows net cash flow of −$160 for May — based on that number alone, can you tell whether May was a good or bad month for the repair work? Three options: (a) Yes, May must have been a bad month; (b) No, since customers paid it must have been a good month; (c) Not from the Cash sheet alone — we need to know what the money came in for and what it went out for.
4. The student selects one option; the Submit button enables once a selection is made. Pressing Enter submits (standard behavior); the Submit control gates advancement.
5. If (c) is chosen: success — feedback says cash isn’t the whole story; some of what went out bought things you still have; some of what came in came with strings attached; the Cash sheet alone can’t tell what happened; go through it line by line, starting with money coming in. The Next -> message unlocks and a Reset button appears.
6. If (a) is chosen: error — a negative cash flow does not automatically mean May was a bad month; some cash that went out bought things still held (repair stand, fixtures, parts on shelf); need to look at what each outflow was for. If (b): error — customer payments came in but that doesn’t automatically mean a good month; some cash comes with strings attached (Smith’s $220 was a deposit for work still to do); need to look at what each inflow was for. The student can Reset and try again.

### If the student is lost
- “What is the Cash sheet showing me?” → Orient: it is the 10 repair-related rows from the bank statement, grouped into Cash in and Cash out. Nudge: expand the Cash in and Cash out groups to see the line items. Stronger hint: the bottom row is NET CASH FLOW (May) = −$160, the month’s flow (cash in minus cash out), not a balance.
- “Is −$160 good or bad?” → Orient: the question asks whether you can tell from that number alone. Nudge: what did the cash that went out buy, and what did the cash that came in come with? Stronger hint: some outflows bought things still held; some inflows came with strings attached — so the number alone can’t decide it.
- “Why is option (c) right and not (a)?” → Orient: (a) treats a negative number as automatically bad. Nudge: but some of what went out bought things you still have. Stronger hint: you need to know what each outflow and inflow was for before judging the month — which is exactly what (c) says.
- “Why isn’t (b) right — customers paid?” → Orient: customers paid, but one payment was a deposit for work not yet done. Nudge: Smith’s $220 came with a string attached. Stronger hint: money coming in is not automatically yours to keep — so customer payments alone don’t prove a good month.

### TUTOR-ONLY answer key
TUTOR-ONLY: Correct option is (c) — “Not from the Cash sheet alone. We need to know what the money came in for and what the money went out for.” Cash in totals $1,340 (eight customer payments: 340+180+80+140+130+220+100+150); cash out totals $1,500 (rent $1,300 + Park Tool stand $200); net = −$160. The tutor must NOT state that personal money covered the shortfall — the student reaches that discovery on their own at this negative tally. The page’s correct feedback: “Cash isn’t the whole story. Some of what went out bought you things you still have. Some of what came in came with strings attached.” Confirm (c) only after a genuine attempt.

### Anchor
anchor: for May. Based on that number alone, can you tell whether May was a good month or a bad month for the repair work?

### Before and after
Before: the student sorted the bank statement into repair-related and personal rows. After: the student opens the customer receipts to learn what each customer payment was for.
