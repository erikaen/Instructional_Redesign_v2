# RAG Knowledge Base — Module 1 (pages 11-x) and Module 2 (pages 12-x)

This file documents, in student order, the pages assigned for the AI Tutor:
`11-1-Getting-Started.html`, `11-2-A-Rough-Week.html`,
`11-3-Sort-Your-Things.html`, `11-4-The-Line-You-Drew.html`,
`11-5-Module-Complete.html`, `12-1-Meet-Maria.html`,
`12-2-Marias-Form.html`, `12-3-The-Read-Back.html`,
`12-4-Module-Complete.html`.

Tutor conduct reminders that govern every “how to help” below: the course
teaches by DISCOVERY — the student experiences each idea before it is named, so
the tutor never announces what a page will reveal. Help means orienting
questions, then progressively stronger hints, mirroring the pages’ own forgiving
three-strike pattern (hint → stronger hint → reveal). The tutor uses the phrase
“accounting identity,” never the banned phrasing for the balancing equation, and
never the disallowed student-facing vocabulary for owner claims or the aggregate
net phrasing. The repair work is an activity, not an actor — it cannot own, owe,
or agree; documents and time periods are not actors
either; records SHOW things, entities HAVE things. Every page has a header with
a progress bar, a glossary button, and a restart button; Enter submits typed
answers; Continue buttons gate advancement and unlock when the page’s task
completes; sorting tasks put items on the left and category zones on the right,
and a “Do the rest” / “Sort the rest” control appears after a few correct
placements. A hidden triple-click dev mode exists but is INSTRUCTOR-ONLY — never
point students to it.

## 11-1-Getting-Started.html — Module 1 — Getting Organized

### What this page is
This is the opening page of the “Getting Organized” arc (the first sub-module
of Module 1). There is no task to perform yet — it sets the scene: the student
has a day job but repairs bikes on the side, demand has grown, and they have
rented a small workspace, which raises real business questions. It previews what
the student will discover (why success forces organization, what the first line
in their records is really about, how the repair work begins to behave like its
own thing) and then gates on a Continue/Next button. It comes before “A Rough
Week,” where three messy incidents make the need concrete.

### The page, step by step
1. The page loads with the header (progress bar, glossary button, restart
   button) and a narrative block: two paragraphs describing the day job, the
   bike repair work spreading by word of mouth, and the rented workspace.
2. Below the narrative is a section card titled “What you’ll discover” with
   three bullet points: why a small success can require better organization;
   what the first line in your records represents and what it is really about;
   how your repair work begins to function like its own business.
3. At the bottom is the message “You’ve finished this section — click Next → to
   continue.” There is nothing to click inside the body — the student reads and
   then advances with the Next button to go to 11-2.

### If the student is lost
- “I don’t know what to do here.”
  - Orient: This page is a setup, not a task. What does it ask you to do?
  - Nudge: It only asks you to read. Is there a button at the bottom?
  - Stronger hint: There is no interaction on this page — read the story and the
    “What you’ll discover” list, then click Next to move on.
- “What is this course about / what does ‘the first line in your records’ mean?”
  - Orient: What does the bullet list say you’ll discover?
  - Nudge: It’s a preview — the page is telling you what to watch for, not asking
    you to know it yet.
  - Stronger hint: Don’t try to answer that now. The course will show you what
    the first line is about; this page just plants the question.
- “Should I open the glossary / click the book icon?”
  - Orient: That button opens the glossary. For this page it has nothing yet —
    terms land as the lists earn their names.
  - Nudge: You can look, but there’s no term to learn on this page.
  - Stronger hint: Skip it for now; just read and click Next.

### TUTOR-ONLY answer key
TUTOR-ONLY: There is no graded answer on this page. The only required action is
reading and then clicking Next →. The three preview bullets are the page’s own
statement of the arc’s discoveries; the tutor must NOT pre-name them. In
particular, do not say what “the first line in your records” turns out to be —
the student draws that line themselves on 11-3. Do not introduce any accounting
term (no “asset,” “entity,” “identity”). The page has no three-strike reveal
because it has no question. Confirm only that the student should advance with
Next.

### Anchor
anchor: What the first line in your records represents and what it is really about

### Before and after
Before: nothing — this is the first page of the module. After: the student
clicks Next to “A Rough Week,” where three incidents make the need to organize

## 11-2-A-Rough-Week.html — Module 1 — A Rough Week

### What this page is
This page makes the need for organization felt through three short scenarios
that the student reveals one at a time. Each is a moment where “keeping it all
in your head” fails: a customer needs a tool the student can’t find; a
duplicate purchase was made because inventory wasn’t tracked; a nearby fire
raises the question of insurance and what’s actually at risk. After the third
card, a callout reframes the three moments as one realization — the first step
is to write down what you’ve got. It comes after the setup on 11-1 and before
the sorting task on 11-3.

### The page, step by step
1. The page shows the narrative line “Three things happened this week. None of
   them went smoothly.” and the first scenario card (“A customer asks”) is
   already revealed.
2. Below the first card is a button labeled “Then what happened next →”. The
   student clicks it to reveal the second scenario card (“It’s not all in your
   head anymore”).
3. Another “Then what happened next →” button appears; clicking it reveals the
   third card (“What if something happened?”).
4. After the third card is revealed, the tail block appears: a callout —
   “Three different moments, one realization: it’s too much to keep in your
   head. If you’re going to get a grip on this, the first step is to write down
   what you’ve got.” — followed by the Next → message. Clicking Next advances to
   11-3.

### If the student is lost
- “What do I do?”
  - Orient: Is there a button on the page right now?
  - Nudge: There’s a “Then what happened next →” button — what happens when you
    click it?
  - Stronger hint: Click that button three times, once for each thing that
    happened this week; the last click shows the wrap-up message.
- “I clicked and only one card showed.”
  - Orient: How many cards have appeared so far?
  - Nudge: Each click reveals one more “what happened next.” Keep going until no
    more buttons appear.
  - Stronger hint: You need three cards total. When the third is up, the summary
    callout and the Next button appear at the bottom.
- “Is there supposed to be a question to answer?”
  - Orient: Does the page ask you to type or choose anything?
  - Nudge: No — these are story cards. Your only action is to reveal them.
  - Stronger hint: This page is about feeling the problem, not answering it. The
    actual task starts on the next page.

### TUTOR-ONLY answer key
TUTOR-ONLY: No graded answer. The student must click “Then what happened next →”
three times to reveal all three scenario cards, which unlocks the tail callout
and the Next button. The three scenarios, in order: (1) “A customer asks” — a
customer needs a 24mm cone wrench the student can’t locate; (2) “It’s not all in
your head anymore” — a duplicate chain whip was bought because inventory wasn’t
tracked; (3) “What if something happened?” — a fire two blocks over raises
insurance and the inability to list everything from memory. The takeaway the
tutor may confirm only after the student has seen it: the first step is to write
down what you’ve got. Do not preview that the next page is a sorting task, and do
not name any accounting concept. There is no three-strike reveal on this page.

### Anchor

## 11-3-Sort-Your-Things.html — Module 1 — Sort Your Things

### What this page is
This is the first hands-on task of the arc. The student’s items are shown in
two locations — the rented space and home — and they must move only the items
that belong with the repair work into a spreadsheet “Things” tab, leaving
personal items out. The catch: some repair-related items (the laptop, the desk,
a box of derailleur cables) are sitting at home, and the student’s own commuter
bike is in the space but is personal. Once the student has placed enough
correctly (at least five shop items, including at least two of the home
“intruders,” and no personal items in the tab), a “Sort the rest →” button
unlocks to file the remaining repair things automatically. It comes before
“11-4-The-Line-You-Drew,” which asks the student to name what the line they drew
is really about.

### The page, step by step
1. The narrative explains: items are in two places (rented space and home); the
   student opens a spreadsheet with a “Things” tab. Instruction: “Tap or drag
   into the Things tab only the items that belong with the repair work. Leave
   everything else where it is. Select the info icon if you’re not sure what an
   item is.”
2. Two location panels (“At the rented space,” “At home”) list item chips on the
   left; the Bike-Repair.xlsx panel with the Things tab is the drop target on the
   right. Each chip has a name (click to place) and an info button (shows the
   item’s description).
3. The student drags or taps repair-related items into the Things tab. Items
   can be removed with the × on each spreadsheet row (until “Sort the rest” is
   used).
4. Live feedback guides placement: a warning if a personal item (the guitar,
   family photos, or the commuter bike) lands in the tab (“… is in the tab, but
   it’s yours. Not part of the repair work. Take it back out.”); “Keep going.
   Move the repair-related items into the tab.” while fewer than five shop items
   are placed; “Some of the repair work is hiding at home, not in the space.
   (What about the laptop?)” once five shop items are in but the home intruders
   aren’t; and a green “You’ve got the idea! … ‘Sort the rest’ will file the
   remaining repair things for you.” once the gate is met (≥5 shop items, ≥2
   home intruders, no personal items in the tab).
5. When the gate is met, the “Sort the rest →” button enables (it reads “Done →”
   if every shop item is already placed). Clicking it files all remaining
   repair-related items and locks the page, revealing the insight box: the
   student pulled things from both places, the laptop and desk came from home
   and neither repairs anything, the commuter bike stayed out even though it was
   in the space — so the box isn’t “stuff in the space” or “tools.” “The next
   question names it.” Then Next → unlocks.

### If the student is lost
- “I don’t know which items go in.”
  - Orient: The instruction says to put in items that belong with the repair
    work. Pick one item — is it part of the repair work, or yours personally?
  - Nudge: Use the info button on a chip if you’re unsure what it is. Then ask:
    does this item relate to the repair work?
  - Stronger hint (page’s own nudge): Some repair work is hiding at home — what
    about the laptop? And your own commuter bike is in the space, but it’s yours,
    not the repair work’s — leave it out.
- “It says take it back out / a personal item is in the tab.”
  - Orient: Which item did the warning name?
  - Nudge: That item is yours personally (the guitar, the photos, or your
    commuter bike). The Things tab is for the repair work only.
  - Stronger hint: Click the × next to that item’s row in the spreadsheet to
    remove it; the warning clears when no personal items remain in the tab.
- “Nothing happens when I drag / the button stays disabled.”
  - Orient: How many repair-related items have you placed, and from which
    location?
  - Nudge: You need at least five repair things in the tab, including some that
    are sitting at home (the laptop, the desk, the cables).
  - Stronger hint: The page is waiting for you to find the repair items that
    live at home — place the laptop and at least one more home item, then “Sort
    the rest” lights up.
- “I put everything in the space in and it still wants more.”
  - Orient: Are all the repair things actually in the rented space?
  - Nudge: Some repair things are at home, not in the space. Look at the “At
    home” panel.
  - Stronger hint: The laptop, the desk, and the box of derailleur cables are at
    home but belong to the repair work — drag those in too.

### TUTOR-ONLY answer key
TUTOR-ONLY: The Things tab must contain all items with `zone:'shop'` and no
items with `zone:'personal'`. There are 22 shop items total across both panels:
all 22 items in the rented-space panel except the guitar, family photos, and the
commuter bike (those three are personal and stay out), plus the three home
“intruders” — the Desk, the Laptop, and the Box of derailleur cables (which are
shop items physically at home). The gate to enable “Sort the rest” is: at least
five shop items placed, at least two of the three home intruders placed, and no
personal items in the tab. The tutor hints first and only confirms after the
student has genuinely attempted; the page’s third-strike-style nudge is “Some of
the repair work is hiding at home, not in the space. (What about the laptop?)”,
then the green confirmation “You’ve got the idea! You found the work-stuff at
home and left your own things out. ‘Sort the rest’ will file the remaining repair
things for you.” The insight box reveal says the box is not “stuff in the space”
and not “tools” — it is something else, and the next question names it. The tutor
must NOT name it here (do not say “assets,” “entity,” “the difference,” or any
accounting term); that naming is for later pages.

### Anchor
anchor: The next question names it.

### Before and after
Before: the student felt the need to write things down on 11-2. After: the
student clicks Next to “The Line You Drew,” which asks them to state in words
what the line they just drew is really about.

## 11-4-The-Line-You-Drew.html — Module 1 — The Line You Drew

### What this page is
This page is a single multiple-choice reflection on what the student just did
on 11-3. The narrative says: “You filled the Things tab from both places, and
left a lot behind. You drew a line. What is the line actually about?” The
student picks the best description of what ended up on the Things tab and
submits; wrong choices get targeted feedback that pushes back toward the right
idea without naming it in accounting language. It comes after the sorting task
and before the Module 1 completion page.

### The page, step by step
1. The narrative block frames the reflection: the student drew a line by
   sorting; what is that line about?
2. A multiple-choice card asks: “Which best describes what ended up on the
   Things tab?” with four options:
   - A: Things related to your repair work
   - B: Things you use for repairing bicycles
   - C: Things in the rented space
   - D: Things the business owns
3. The student clicks an option to select it; the Submit button enables. The
   student clicks Submit.
4. If correct (A): a success sound plays, the chosen option is marked correct,
   the green feedback gives the full answer, and the insight box “The line you
   drew” appears, followed by Next →.
5. If wrong: a “nope” sound plays, the chosen option is marked wrong, and a
   targeted feedback message appears (different for B, C, and D) explaining why
   that description doesn’t fit, pushing the student to try again. A Reset
   button appears so they can re-choose and re-submit. The student keeps trying
   until they select A.

### If the student is lost
- “I picked one and it said ‘not quite’ — what now?”
  - Orient: Which option did you pick, and what did the feedback say was wrong
    with it?
  - Nudge: Each wrong option is wrong for a specific reason the feedback names.
    Read it, then look at the other options.
  - Stronger hint (page’s feedback): B is wrong because the laptop and desk
    don’t repair anything but still went in; C is wrong because the laptop and
    desk were at home and your commuter bike was in the space but stayed out; D
    is wrong because there’s no separate business yet — you personally own
    everything. The line is about whether things relate to the repair work.
- “I keep getting it wrong.”
  - Orient: Use Reset, then re-read the four choices against what you actually
    did on the last page.
  - Nudge: You put the laptop and desk in even though they were at home and
    don’t fix bikes, and you left your commuter bike out even though it was in
    the space. Which choice explains that?
  - Stronger hint: The right choice is the one that’s only about whether
    something is part of the repair work — not where it is, not what it’s used
    for, not who owns it.
- “Is D right — ‘Things the business owns’?”
  - Orient: Is there a separate business entity at this point in the story?
  - Nudge: You personally own the stand, the laptop, the couch, and the bike.
    Ownership alone doesn’t explain why some went in and some stayed out.
  - Stronger hint (page’s feedback): There is no separate business entity yet.
    The better description is that the items in the tab are the ones related to
    your repair work.

### TUTOR-ONLY answer key
TUTOR-ONLY: The correct option is **A — “Things related to your repair work.”**
The page’s correct-answer feedback: “Things related to your repair work. The
stand, the parts, and the laptop on your desk at home all relate to the repair
work, wherever they sit and whatever their job is. The guitar, the couch, and
your own commuter bike do not. That line is the most important thing you drew
today.” Wrong-option feedback (the page’s own hints):
- B: “Not quite. You don’t repair anything with the laptop or the desk — they
  hold the schedule, the orders, and the paperwork — but they still went in. It
  isn’t about what you use to fix bikes.”
- C: “Not quite. The laptop and the desk were sitting at home, not in the space.
  Your own commuter bike was right there in the space, but it stayed out.”
- D: “Careful. There is no separate business entity yet. You personally own the
  repair stand, laptop, couch, and commuter bike. Ownership alone does not
  explain why some items went into the Things tab and others stayed out.”
The insight box (“The line you drew”) states: the line is about whether
something is part of the repair work or not — not where it is, not what it’s
used for, not who “owns” it. The tutor confirms A only after genuine attempts and
must not translate it into accounting vocabulary (no “asset,” “entity boundary,”
“the difference”). There is no automatic third-strike reveal beyond the
wrong-option feedback; the student re-submits until correct.

### Anchor
anchor: Which best describes what ended up on the Things tab?

### Before and after
Before: the student sorted items into the Things tab on 11-3. After: the student
clicks Next to the Module 1 completion page, which recaps the discovery and
previews Module 2.

## 12-2-Marias-Form.html — Module 2 — Maria’s Form

### What this page is
This is the main task page of Module 2. Maria’s form has four sections —
Equipment, Computer Equipment, Supplies, Fixtures — and the student drags each
item from the Things list into the correct section, then adds a dollar amount to
each. The page runs in two phases: first categorize (place items by type), then
valuate (type dollar amounts). Most values come off a receipt (open the
receipt/card document), the older laptop uses an eBay comp, and supplies require
a handwritten shelf count because Maria insures only what’s still on the shelf.
Once the student has correctly placed and valued at least one item in every
section, a “Do the rest →” button unlocks to finish the remaining lines
automatically. It comes after 12-1 and before “The Read-Back” (12-3).

### The page, step by step
1. The narrative says the form has four sections and instructs the student to
   drag each item into the correct section. A sidebar on the left (“YOUR THINGS
   LIST”) shows six visible items; the form panel on the right shows the four
   empty sections and a “TOTAL INSURABLE PROPERTY” line.
2. Categorize phase: the student drags (or selects then clicks a section) each
   visible item into its section. Wrong section placements get a red nudge
   naming the correct category (e.g., “Laptop is Computer Equipment — a
   computer or device, kept separate from your hand tools.”); correct placements
   get a green confirmation. Six visible items cover all four sections: Park
   Tool repair stand → Equipment, Air compressor → Equipment, Laptop → Computer
   Equipment, Box of chains → Supplies, Pegboard + hooks → Fixtures, Desk →
   Fixtures.
3. Once every section has at least one item, Maria’s transition nudge appears:
   she explains the insurer pays what a desk is worth, not a new desk, so she
   needs a dollar amount on each item. An “Add dollar values →” button appears;
   clicking it switches to the valuate phase and updates the narrative.
4. Valuate phase: each item row shows an evidence button (receipt/card/eBay comp)
   and a dollar input. The student opens the document to read the amount, then
   types it and presses Enter (or blurs the field). Correct values show green;
   wrong values show red. For the laptop, Maria’s nudge appears: it has no
   receipt, so use the eBay comp (comparable sold prices clustering around
   $610). For supplies, opening the QBP receipt shows what was paid for whole
