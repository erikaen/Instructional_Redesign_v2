## 45-1-What-Cash-Missed.html — What Cash Missed
### What this page is
This is the first page of Module 4's closing arc (Tutorial 5, Page 1 of 1, ~10 min). It walks the student from the income statement's net income ($2,640) to the cash statement's operating cash ($2,350) for the same season, one timing or non-cash item at a time. It comes after the four statements have been built and before the statements are shown to tie out together in 46-1. The lesson the student experiences here — before it is named — is that the two statements answer different questions: one times to the work, one times to the cash.

### The page, step by step
The header shows the title "What Cash Missed," the sub-line "Module 4 · The Angel Investor · ~10 min · Page 1 of 1," and the glossary and restart buttons. A narrative block opens with Anna asking the student to walk from net income ($2,640) to operating cash ($2,350), and a paragraph stating that three items sit between those two numbers.

Below that, a gap-track table titled "Net income vs. Operating cash" begins with a single row — Net income $2,640 — and a "Running toward Operating cash" total also at $2,640. The page then moves through three items in sequence. For each item, Anna poses a question, and a Continue button reads "See what it does to the gap →." Clicking it reveals an insight box for that item and adds a signed row to the track, then a new Continue button appears ("Next item →," or "State the lesson →" on the last one).

The three items, in order: (1) Ridgeline Trail Crew — $300, revenue this season with cash still to come, shown as −$300; (2) Smith's custom frame — $220, revenue this season but cash arrived last period as a deposit, shown as −$220; (3) Depreciation — $230, an expense that is never cash, added back as +$230. The running total walks $2,640 → $2,340 → $2,120 → $2,350.

After the third item, Anna states that the gap is fully explained — $2,640 minus $300 minus $220 plus $230 lands exactly on $2,350 — and notes the $2,030 card payoff sits elsewhere, under Financing, which is why these two operating numbers can be compared clean. An insight box "Two honest clocks" explains the income statement times to the work and the Cash list times to the cash, and Anna attributes the phrase "same shop, two clocks" to Freddie. A final Continue button reads "See them hang together →" and navigates to 46-1.

### If the student is lost
- "Why is net income bigger than operating cash?" Orient: which statement times things to the work, and which times things to the cash? Nudge: each of the three items is a place where the two answer differently — one is timing, one is timing the other way, one never moves cash at all. Stronger hint: Ridgeline's $300 is revenue now with cash still to come (a receivable); Smith's $220 is revenue now but the cash came last period; depreciation lowered net income with no cash leaving.
- "Where does the $2,030 card payoff fit?" Orient: is that payoff an operating item or a financing item? Nudge: it settled a borrowing from Module 2; the costs behind it were already expensed when the things were used. Stronger hint: the page places it under Financing on the cash statement, never between net income and operating cash — that is why the two operating numbers compare clean.
- "Why is depreciation added back?" Orient: did any cash leave the shop to record it? Nudge: it is an allocation, not a payment, so the Cash list has nothing to show. Stronger hint: it pulled net income down $230 with no matching cash outflow, so to reconcile the gap it gets added back.
- Tutor conduct: let the student click "See what it does to the gap →" themselves; the page reveals each insight. Do not announce "two clocks" or "articulation" before the page does. Use "accounting identity" language if the identity comes up; do not use the words the course avoids.

### TUTOR-ONLY answer key
TUTOR-ONLY — confirm only after the student has attempted each step.
- Item 1, Ridgeline: sign −1, $300. Running: $2,640 → $2,340. Insight: "Revenue this season, cash still to come."
- Item 2, Smith: sign −1, $220. Running: $2,340 → $2,120. Insight: "Revenue this season, cash arrived last period."
- Item 3, Depreciation: sign +1, $230. Running: $2,120 → $2,350. Insight: "An expense that is never cash … added back."
- The page has no typed third-strike reveal; the final Anna statement is the reveal: the gap is fully explained, $2,640 − $300 − $220 + $230 = $2,350, and the card payoff belongs under Financing.
- "Two honest clocks" insight and Freddie's "same shop, two clocks" phrase are stated by the page, not by the tutor first.

### Anchor
anchor: Three items sit between those two numbers — each one a place where the two answer differently.

### Before and after
Before: the student built the income statement, the statement of changes in Members' Capital, the cash-flow statement, and the walk from net income toward operating cash. After: 46-1 shows the statements tying out as one picture.

## 46-1-The-Statements-Tie-Out.html — The Statements Tie Out
### What this page is
Tutorial 6, Page 1 of 2 (~10 min). The student matches each of three balance-sheet changes to the statement that explains it, using a workbook whose tabs show the actual statements. The page names "articulation" only after all three are matched. It follows the net-income-to-operating-cash reconciliation in 45-1 and precedes the whole-map view in 46-2.

### The page, step by step
The header reads "The Statements Tie Out" with sub "Page 1 of 2." A narrative block says every statement now exists and that three things moved between the two balance-sheet snapshots; the student must find, for each, the statement that carries the story.

A workbook is mounted below with the Aug-31 snapshot plus statement tabs (income, bridge, cashflows, balance); it opens on the balance tab showing the June 1 and August 31 snapshots side by side. The student can click tabs to inspect each statement.

A section card "Which statement explains it?" holds three multiple-choice cards. For each, the student clicks an option to select it (the Submit button is disabled until one is chosen), then clicks Submit. A correct answer plays a success sound, locks the choice, and shows a green feedback explanation; a wrong answer plays a nope tone, shows a red feedback explanation, and offers a "Reset" button so the student can try again.

The three prompts and options: (1) "Cash fell $680 (from $1,300 to $620)" — options The Income Statement / The Statement of Cash Flows / The Statement of Changes in Members' Capital; (2) "Members' Capital rose $2,040 (from $2,460 to $4,500)" — options The Statement of Changes in Members' Capital / The Statement of Cash Flows / The Income Statement alone; (3) "Generated (inside Members' Capital) rose from $170 to $2,810" — options The Statement of Changes in Members' Capital / The Statement of Cash Flows alone / Contributed — new money put in.

Once all three are correct, Anna's completion dialog appears and names "articulation" — the statements tie to each other; the balance sheet shows where things stand, the flow statements show how it got there. An insight box "Articulation" reinforces this, and a Continue button "Next: the whole map →" navigates to 46-2. Before completion, Anna's prompt simply tells the student to pick, for each move, the statement that carries the story, not just the new number.

### If the student is lost
- "Which statement explains the Cash change?" Orient: which statement is built to explain a change in Cash? Nudge: it has Operating, Investing, and Financing sections. Stronger hint: the page's wrong-answer feedback notes that the change in Cash has its own statement — the Statement of Cash Flows (here +$2,350, −$400, −$2,630 = −$680).
- "Why isn't the Members' Capital change explained by the Income Statement alone?" Orient: what else moved through Members' Capital besides net income? Nudge: the owner's draw also passed through. Stronger hint: only the bridge statement carries all three piles — Contributed, Generated, and Withdrawn — so it is the one that ties $2,460 to $4,500.
- "Is Generated the same as Contributed?" Orient: did any new money get contributed this season? Nudge: Contributed never moved; Generated is built from what the work produced. Stronger hint: the page's feedback states Contributed never moved this season; Generated is built from net income, not from new money put in.
- Encourage the student to click the workbook tabs to inspect each statement before answering. Tutor conduct: do not name "articulation" before the page does; let the wrong-answer feedback do its work.

### TUTOR-ONLY answer key
TUTOR-ONLY — confirm only after the student has submitted a genuine attempt; allow the Reset path for wrong answers.
- Card 1 (Cash −$680): correct answer B — The Statement of Cash Flows. Correct feedback: "Operating +$2,350, Investing −$400, Financing −$2,630, net −$680."
- Card 2 (Members' Capital +$2,040): correct answer A — The Statement of Changes in Members' Capital. Correct feedback: "Contributed $2,290 (unchanged), Generated $170 + net income $2,640 = $2,810, Withdrawn ($600) — the three piles sum to $4,500."
- Card 3 (Generated $170 → $2,810): correct answer A — The Statement of Changes in Members' Capital. Correct feedback: "beginning $170 + net income $2,640 = ending $2,810; Withdrawn ($600) is its own pile, never folded into Generated."
- There is no typed third-strike reveal; the mechanism is MCQ with per-card Reset until correct, then the completion dialog reveals "articulation."

### Anchor
anchor: Three things moved between the two balance-sheet snapshots. For each one, find the statement that carries the story.

### Before and after
Before: 45-1 reconciled net income to operating cash across three gap items. After: 46-2 lays all three paths out as one map.

## 46-2-The-Statement-Map.html — Three Paths and the Map
### What this page is
Tutorial 6, Page 2 of 2 (~8 min). A visual map places the June 1 and August 31 balance-sheet snapshots side by side and reveals, one path at a time, the flow statement that carries each change — Cash, Members' Capital, and the Net income nested inside Members' Capital's story — then shows the whole set at once. It follows the matching exercise in 46-1 and precedes Freddie's forward-looking napkin in 47-1.

### The page, step by step
The header reads "Three Paths and the Map" with sub "Page 2 of 2." Navigation is Back/Next across five steps (0–4); the page scrolls to top on each move.

Step 0: both balance sheets render fully (June 1 and August 31). A center card "The Question" says the identity holds at both dates but the snapshots alone do not say how one became the other. Freddie asks to be walked through how the set fits. Next is enabled.

Step 1 (Cash path): Freddie asks to start with Cash; the Cash line and Total Assets stay unfaded while the rest of each sheet fades. The center shows the Statement of Cash Flows: Operating +$2,350, Investing −$400, Financing −$2,630, net −$680, tying $1,300 to $620. A narrative notes Financing holds both the card payoff and the owner's draw.

Step 2 (Members' Capital path): Freddie asks to see the Members' Capital path; Contributed, Generated, Withdrawn, and Total Members' Capital stay unfaded. The center shows the Statement of Changes in Members' Capital. The narrative walks the three piles: Contributed $2,290 unchanged; Generated $170 + net income $2,640 = $2,810; Withdrawn ($600); totals tie $2,460 to $4,500.

Step 3 (Net income path): Freddie calls net income the biggest line in that bridge and asks what is behind it. The narrative tells the student to click the Net income line below to open the Income Statement behind it. In the center bridge statement, the Net income row is clickable (a pulsing chevron marks it); clicking toggles a nested Income Statement showing Revenue $6,020 minus Expenses $3,380 = $2,640. Next is disabled until the student opens this nested statement at least once (the "incomeSeen" gate). When open, a note explains that in actual reporting these are two distinct statements.

Step 4 (the whole map): both balance sheets render fully again. The center shows both the Statement of Cash Flows and the Statement of Changes in Members' Capital, with the Income Statement nested open inside the latter. An insight "The rest of the map" notes every other moved line has its story here too — Credit Card to $0 (an Operating line), Customer Deposit to $0 (Smith's frame delivered, inside Revenue), Accounts Receivable appeared (work finished, not yet collected, inside Revenue). Freddie says he now sees where things stand and how they got there; Anna says the set is tied together. A "Coming Next" card previews Freddie talking terms, and a Continue button navigates to 47-1.

A dual identity bar at the bottom shows the accounting identity at both dates: June 1, Assets $4,710 − Liabilities $2,250 = Members' Capital $2,460; August 31, Assets $4,500 − Liabilities $0 = Members' Capital $4,500.

### If the student is lost
- "Why are most lines faded out?" Orient: which lines is this step focused on? Nudge: only the path being walked right now stays bright; the rest fade so the eye follows the story. Stronger hint: in step 1 the bright lines are Cash and Total Assets; in step 2 they are the Members' Capital piles.
- "What do I click at step 3?" Orient: look in the center bridge statement for the row marked with a pulsing chevron. Nudge: the Net income row is the clickable one. Stronger hint: clicking it opens the Income Statement that sits behind that single number.
- "Why is Next disabled at step 3?" Orient: has the page asked you to open something first? Nudge: the Net income line opens the Income Statement behind it. Stronger hint: Next unlocks only after you have opened that nested Income Statement at least once.
- "How do the two snapshots connect?" Orient: what does the identity bar at the bottom show at each date? Nudge: the accounting identity holds at both dates; the flow statements carry the change between them. Stronger hint: each change on a balance sheet travels through one of the flow statements — that is what tying out means.
- Tutor conduct: do not name "articulation" before the page does (46-1 already named it); here the page restates it. Do not blurt the nested Income Statement figures before the student opens it. Use "accounting identity," not the phrase the course avoids.

## 47-1-Freddies-Napkin.html — Freddie's Napkin
### What this page is
Tutorial 7, Page 1 of 1 (~6 min). Freddie sketches a forward-looking, per-month "back-of-the-envelope" picture for an investor scenario; the student reveals the napkin's lines one Continue at a time and then types the bottom line, free cash flow. The page teaches the separation of record (the statements, for the past) from expectation (the napkin, for a future guess), and that salary paid to the repair person as an employee is an expense of the shop, distinct from the owner's draw. It follows the tied-out whole map in 46-2 and opens the move toward Freddie deciding terms.

### The page, step by step
The header reads "Freddie's Napkin," sub "Page 1 of 1." Navigation is Back/Continue across four steps (0–3).

Step 0: Freddie says that if he comes in, the bench gets paid properly — a $2,000/month salary to the repair person as an employee, an expense of the shop like rent or parts. Anna adds the "same two hats" point: one person can be an owner under the Operating Agreement and an employee at the bench; Freddie is putting a number on the employee hat. Continue advances.

Step 1: Freddie supposes the ramped-up shop does $10,000 of repair work a month and sketches it. A napkin card appears titled "FREDDIE'S BACK-OF-THE-ENVELOPE — PER MONTH," starting with the Revenue line ($10,000) already shown. Each click of Continue adds the next line: Parts −$2,000, Rent −$650, Salary −$2,000, Replacement tools & equipment −$350. After the second line appears, Freddie notes Parts ran about a fifth of revenue during the season and he is using that same rough share. After the fifth line appears, Freddie notes gear wears out and gets replaced (carried as cash going out), and names "free cash flow" as the cash that could be paid out and not hurt the shop's business. Once all five rows are shown, a "Free cash flow" input box appears with the prompt to type Freddie's bottom line for a month like this, plus a Check button. The student types a number and presses Enter or clicks Check; Continue stays disabled until the answer is correct.

Step 2: Freddie stresses the napkin is an expectation, not a record — no salary happened last season, nothing on the napkin is a recorded number, and nothing in the season's statements was a promise about any of it; if the ramp-up never comes, the napkin is worth nothing. Anna says to use the statements for the past and the napkin for a future guess, and to keep those two jobs separate.

Step 3: Freddie says months like that would be worth sharing and asks on what terms. A "Coming Next" card previews that Freddie must decide what he is buying and what the deal looks like. This is the final step; only Back is offered.

The Check button uses a three-strike-style hint ladder: on the first wrong attempt it says "Try again. Start with 10,000 and subtract the four costs"; on the second and later attempts it gives the stronger hint "Try again: 10,000 − 2,000 − 650 − 2,000 − 350." The page does not auto-fill the answer; the student must type 5,000 (the $ and commas are ignored). Back at step 0 navigates to 46-2; Back at step 1 before the answer is correct removes napkin lines one at a time.

### If the student is lost
- "Is the salary an expense or a draw?" Orient: which hat is Freddie wearing when the bench gets paid — owner, or employee? Nudge: salary here is pay for work, an expense of the shop like rent or parts; the draw is a separate owner move. Stronger hint: same two hats — owner under the Operating Agreement and employee at the bench; the $2,000/month is the employee hat, so it is an expense and it does touch net income, while the draw never does.
- "How do I get the bottom line?" Orient: start with the revenue and subtract the four costs. Nudge: 10,000 minus 2,000 minus 650 minus 2,000 minus 350. Stronger hint: the page's stronger prompt gives the full arithmetic — 10,000 − 2,000 − 650 − 2,000 − 350 — which is 5,000; the student must still type it.
- "Is the napkin a statement?" Orient: did any of these numbers happen last season? Nudge: the statements record what happened; the napkin is a guess about a future that may not come. Stronger hint: nothing on the napkin is a recorded number and nothing in the season's statements was a promise about any of it.
- Tutor conduct: let the page name "free cash flow" before you do; mirror the page's three-strike pacing and only confirm $5,000 after a genuine attempt. Use "accounting identity" if the identity comes up; do not use the words the course avoids. Do not point students to any hidden dev mode.

### TUTOR-ONLY answer key
TUTOR-ONLY — confirm only after the student has typed a genuine attempt.
- Napkin lines in order: Revenue $10,000; Parts −$2,000; Rent −$650; Salary −$2,000; Replacement tools & equipment −$350.
- Free cash flow (the typed answer) = $5,000 (accepted as 5000; $, commas, and spaces are stripped before comparing).
- First wrong attempt feedback: "Try again. Start with 10,000 and subtract the four costs." Second-and-later (stronger) feedback: "Try again: 10,000 − 2,000 − 650 − 2,000 − 350." The page gives no further auto-reveal; the student must enter 5,000.
- On correct entry the napkin shows "$5,000" and the note "an expectation, not a record."
- The page does not record or alter last season; the salary never happened in the statements, and the napkin is worth nothing if the ramp-up never comes.

### Anchor
anchor: Type Freddie’s bottom line for a month like this.

### Before and after
