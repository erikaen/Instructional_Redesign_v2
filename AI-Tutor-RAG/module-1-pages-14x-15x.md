# Module 1 (pages 14x–15x) — RAG knowledge for the AI Tutor

Tutor conduct encoded below: the course teaches by discovery, so the tutor
orients with questions first, then nudges, then gives a stronger hint —
mirroring each page's three-strike (hint, stronger hint, reveal) pacing. The
tutor never announces what a page is about to reveal. Words the tutor uses:
"accounting identity" (never "accounting equation"), "the difference" (never a
name for Assets minus Liabilities here), "contributed" and "generated" (never
revenue/expenses, never "closing the books"). The repair work is an activity,
not an actor — it does not own, owe, or agree; documents and time periods are
not actors either. Records SHOW things; entities HAVE things. Every page has a
header with a progress bar, a glossary button, and a restart button; Enter
submits typed answers; Continue buttons unlock when the page's task completes.

## 14-1-Why-Keep-the-Reasons.html — Why Keep the Reasons

### What this page is
This is the welcome page that opens the "Reasons" arc. The student already has
an Assets list and a Liabilities list from earlier modules; this page names the
nagging problem — a total tells you *what* moved, never *why* — and sets up the
idea of writing a reason next to every change, on its own tab. Nothing is
unlocked by doing; the student reads and clicks Next. The interactive walk
begins on the next page (14-2).

### The page, step by step
1. A narrative block: Anna is back; the workbook has two lists (Assets,
   Liabilities) that keep changing, and the numbers show *what* the total is
   now but never *why* it moved.
2. The workbook appears with three tabs — Assets, Liabilities, Cash. There is
   **no Reasons tab yet** (it appears later). The student may click tabs and
   expand categories with the +/− toggles to look, but there is no task to
   complete here.
3. Anna's dialog: every time a number on one of these lists changes, jot down
   the reason next to it — give it its own tab.
4. A "What You'll Discover" card lists three bullets (the amount tells you
   what moved / only the reason tells you what happened; asking "what else
   moved?" reveals a match; the changes with no match move the difference and
   sort into two plain kinds).
5. A finish message: "You've finished this section — click Next -> to
   continue." The student clicks Next to advance.

### If the student is lost
- "I don't get what the page wants me to do."
  - Orient: There is no input on this page — what is Anna asking you to start
    doing in your own workbook?
  - Nudge: She says to write something *next to* every change. What would that
    be?
  - Stronger: Re-read Anna's line — "jot down the reason next to it. Give it
    its own tab." That is the whole setup; the doing starts next page.
- "What are the three bullets about — isn't that the answer?"
  - Orient: They are a preview, not a task. Which one surprises you?
  - Nudge: Notice the third bullet says the no-match changes "sort into two
    plain kinds." You will sort them yourself in a few pages.
  - Stronger: For now just hold the question — what makes the difference move?
    You'll find out by doing, not by reading this card.

### TUTOR-ONLY answer key
TUTOR-ONLY: This page has no graded answer. The only gate is clicking Next
after reading. The three preview bullets are the roadmap for the arc: (1)
amount = what moved, reason = what happened; (2) most changes come with a
match; (3) the no-match changes move the difference and sort into two kinds
(contributed and generated, named later). The tutor does not name those kinds
here — the page does not. There is no three-strike reveal on this page.

### Anchor
anchor: Give it its own tab.

### Before and after
Before: earlier modules produced the Assets and Liabilities lists.
Next: 14-2, where the student walks every May change and asks "what else
moved?" for each one.

## 14-2-Walk-the-Reasons.html — Walk the Changes

### What this page is
This is the core interactive walk. For every change on the Assets and
Liabilities sheets, the student asks "what else moved?" and classifies it as
matched (the Credit Card, Cash, or a promise moved by the same amount) or as a
reason (nothing else moved — write it on the Reasons tab). The walk ends with
the tally discovery on Cash: the flows sum to −$160, yet Cash on hand reads
$0, so $160 must have arrived from outside the repair work. The page teaches
the rule: every change gets a match or a reason.

### The page, step by step
1. Two workbook panes side by side: left pane (Assets / Liabilities tabs), right
   pane (the new Reasons tab). A "rule strip" tracks Matched count and Reasons
   total.
2. Anna opens each beat in turn: Equipment, Computer Equipment, Fixtures,
   Supplies, Right of Use, Smith (on the Liabilities tab), and finally Cash.
   The active category auto-expands.
3. For each change row in the active category, the student clicks the row. A
   card appears: "what else moved?" with four buttons — "The Credit Card — by
   the same amount", "Cash — by the same amount", "A promise — by the same
   amount", "Nothing else moved — write a reason". A "See the evidence" button
   opens a receipt/evidence modal for that transaction.
4. Correct choice: a success sound and a flash explaining what happened to the
   difference (matched → difference didn't move; reason → the difference
   moved). Wrong: a nudge; after three wrong attempts the page records the
   correct answer (three-strike reveal).
5. After enough rows in a beat are recorded (and both a match and a reason have
   appeared, when the beat is mixed), a "Walk the rest" button auto-completes
   the remaining rows of that beat. Then "Next — <category>" advances.
6. On the Smith beat (Liabilities), the promise-up-$220 is matched by Cash
   up $220. Anna notes the day Smith gets the frame, the promise drops to zero
   with nothing moving with it — that future change will need a reason.
7. Final beat — Cash: most Cash rows are already matched (check marks). The
   remaining rows are the money that came in (customer payments). After they
   are recorded, the tally discovery appears: the rows sum to −$160 but Cash
   on hand is $0. Three buttons ask where the $160 came from. The correct
   choice is "It came from outside the repair work — you covered it yourself,
   without noticing." The $160 "cover" row is added as the last reason.
8. Finish: two insight boxes ("The reasons are the record of the difference";
   "Two kinds, coming up") and a "Click Next to sort them" message.

### If the student is lost
- "Which button do I press for this row?"
  - Orient: Look at the evidence (receipt) for this row — who paid, and how?
  - Nudge: A Visa charge means the Credit Card moved; a debit card or check
    means Cash moved; "brought from home — no purchase" means nothing else
    moved.
  - Stronger: That is exactly the page's hint 2/2 — match the payment method
    to the button. If still unsure, three strikes will reveal it.
- "Why did the difference move on this one but not the last one?"
  - Orient: Did anything else move by the same amount at the same time?
  - Nudge: If yes, the two changes cancel on the difference; if no, the
    difference itself moved and you write a reason.
  - Stronger: That is the rule — every change gets a match or a reason. A
    match leaves the difference alone; a reason moves it.
- The tally discovery ("$160 missing"):
  - Orient: The rows sum to −$160 but the account never went negative. What
    does that imply?
  - Nudge: Every customer payment already has a row, and the card's charges
    are all matched. Where else could money come from?
  - Stronger: It came from outside the repair work — you covered it yourself.
    Pick the third option. (The page reveals this as the third strike / the
    correct answer.)

### TUTOR-ONLY answer key
TUTOR-ONLY: Per-change answers — Equipment: stand→Cash (match); six hand
tools→Credit Card (match, one $300 charge); compressor/washer/truing→Credit
Card (match); grinder, headset press, shop vacuum→Nothing else (reason:
brought from home). Laptop→Nothing else (reason: brought from home). Fixtures
(four lines)→Credit Card (one $750 charge, match). Supplies bought→Credit Card
(one $500 charge, match); Supplies used (7 negative rows)→Nothing else

Cash rows: rent/stand already matched; seven repair payments→Nothing else
(reason each); Smith deposit cash side already matched. Tally discovery: the
$160 came from the personal self (option c). The page's third-strike/stronger
hint for the tally says: every customer payment has a row and a reason
already; the card's charges are all matched; this $160 wasn't borrowed. The
reasons total +$1,160 (Contributed $990 + Generated $170) which equals Assets
$3,410 − Liabilities $2,250. The tutor confirms only after a genuine attempt.

### Anchor
anchor: Same question, row by row: what else moved?

## 14-4-What-Moves-the-Difference.html — What Moves the Difference

### What this page is
This page opens already past the walk: every matched pair has been crossed out,
leaving only the "solo" reasons (the changes with no match). The student sorts
each reason into one of two bins — Contributed (put in from outside the repair
work) or Generated (came from doing the work). The page delivers the payoff:
the difference grows in exactly two ways.

### The page, step by step
1. The page loads straight into the "movers" phase. The Reasons tab shows the
   unpaired reason rows as cards (account, signed amount, reason text).
2. Anna explains the two kinds: some you put in yourself from outside the
   repair work (the laptop and gear from home, and the $160 your personal self
   quietly covered) — these are **contributed**; the rest came from doing the
   work (what customers paid, less the parts and the month of workspace used up)
   — these are **generated**.
3. Each card has two buttons: "→ Contributed" and "→ Generated". The student
   clicks the correct bin for each row.
4. Correct: success sound, the row lands in its bin (the bin's count and total
   update). Wrong: a nudge message ("That one didn't come from you bringing
   something in — it came from doing the repair work. Try the other bin." or
   the reverse).
5. After two or more correct placements, a "Sort the rest" button appears and
   auto-finishes the remaining rows.
6. When all rows are sorted, Anna summarizes: Contributed total and Generated
   total, and "That figure is what the repair work produced on its own in
   May." An insight box "Two ways the difference grew" breaks down both kinds.
   Then "Finish up" → finish message (Next to continue).

### If the student is lost
- "Which bin does this row go in?"
  - Orient: Where did this change come from — from outside the repair work, or
    from doing the work?
  - Nudge: Anything brought from home (laptop, gear, the $160 your personal
    self covered) came from outside — that is contributed. Customer payments
    and the parts/space used up came from doing the work — generated.
  - Stronger: If the row is a customer payment or a "used $X of parts" or
    "used the space for May," it is generated. If it is "brought from home" or
    "personal self covered," it is contributed. The wrong-bin nudge will tell
    you which way to switch.
- "Why are the parts-used rows negative and in Generated?"
  - Orient: Doing the work used up parts. Did that come from outside, or from
    the work itself?
  - Nudge: The work consumed them — so they belong with doing the work, even
    though the amount is negative.
  - Stronger: Generated is what the work made, after subtracting what it
    consumed. The negative parts and the used-up month are the subtracting.

### TUTOR-ONLY answer key
TUTOR-ONLY: Contributed (5 rows, total +$990) — Cash +$160 (personal self
covered), Laptop +$610, Equipment +$90 (bench grinder), +$70 (headset press),
+$60 (shop vacuum). Generated (14 rows, total +$170) — seven customer Cash
payments +$340, +$180, +$80, +$140, +$130, +$100, +$150; seven Supplies-used
rows −$70, −$50, −$30, −$50, −$30, −$30, −$40; Right of Use used for May −$650.
Combined reasons total = $1,160 = Assets $3,410 − Liabilities $2,250. The page
has no third-strike reveal for the sort (wrong choices just nudge to the other
bin); the tutor confirms totals only after genuine attempts.

### Anchor
anchor: Sort each row into the right bin by clicking on the button in each row.

### Before and after
Before: 14-2 walked every change and produced the Reasons tab; the matched
pairs were crossed out.
Next: 14-5 recaps the module, then Module 5 lays everything on one page and
earns the names.

## 15-3-The-Entity.html — The Entity

### What this page is
This page steps back to the very first move — drawing a line between "this is
the repair work" and "this is me" — and generalizes it. The student answers
Yes/No for six candidates: could a clear edge be drawn around it, for a
purpose, and a set of books kept about it? The page then names the subject of
any set of books: the **entity** — a perspective adopted for a purpose, not an
object found in the world.

### The page, step by step
1. Narrative: in Module 1 the student drew a line — this stand, this card, this
   slice of the bank account is the repair work; groceries, gas, paycheck are
   not. The question is turned around: what else could there be a set of books
   about?
2. A quiz of six cards, each with "Yes — there could be" and "No" buttons:
   1. Your repair work — Yes.
   2. You — the person, apart from the repair work — Yes.
   3. The food bank on Whalley Ave — Yes.
   4. The bicycle industry — Yes.
   5. Smith's $220 deposit — No.
   6. Last May — No.
3. Each answer shows feedback (correct/incorrect with an explanation). Buttons
   disable after answering.
4. After all six are answered, Anna reveals: anything with a clear edge drawn
   around it, for a purpose, can be the subject of a set of books — that
   subject is the **entity**. "You didn't find the repair work's edge. You
   made it." An insight box "The boundary is the work; the name follows" says
   drawing the line in the wrong place makes every number after it answer the
   wrong question.
5. Finish message: "You've finished this section — click Next -> to continue."

### If the student is lost
- "How do I decide Yes or No for these?"
  - Orient: Could someone draw a clear edge around it, for a purpose, and keep
    records about what falls inside?
  - Nudge: An event that happened (like a deposit) lands *in* a set of books —
    it doesn't keep records. A stretch of time is what books *cover*, not what
    they are about.
  - Stronger: Ask "is this a thing someone could draw a boundary around for a
    purpose?" Yes for the repair work, the person, the food bank, the bicycle
    industry; No for the deposit (an event) and Last May (a time period).
- "Why is 'You — the person' a Yes?"
  - Orient: You drew a line in Module 1 with the repair work on one side. What
    was on the other side?
  - Nudge: The same sorting can point the other way — a person can have a set
    of books too.
  - Stronger: The books don't care why the thing exists, only where its edge
    is. A person has an edge; groceries and paycheck fall outside the repair
    work's edge but inside the person's.
- "Why is the bicycle industry a Yes but it has no owner?"
  - Orient: Does keeping books require an owner, or just someone whose purpose
    draws the edge?
  - Nudge: A trade association or market-research firm can bound "thousands of
    shops, makers, and riders" for a purpose — sizing, benchmarking, lobbying.
  - Stronger: No owner needed — same rule as the food bank: someone draws the
    edge for a purpose and keeps records about what is inside.

### TUTOR-ONLY answer key
TUTOR-ONLY: Answers — Your repair work: Yes. You (the person, apart from the
repair work): Yes. The food bank on Whalley Ave: Yes. The bicycle industry:
Yes. Smith's $220 deposit: No (it is an event that lands in a set of books —
cash up, a promise up — not something books are kept about). Last May: No (a
stretch of time; books belong to a subject and cover a time, so May is the
period covered, not a subject). The reveal names the **entity** — a
perspective adopted for a purpose, not an object in the world; the boundary is
made, not found. The page gives immediate per-card feedback, so there is no
three-strike sequence; the tutor confirms the reasoning, not just the label.

### Anchor
anchor: For each one below, decide: could a clear edge be drawn around it, for a purpose — and a set of books kept about it?

## 15-4-Double-Entry.html — Double-Entry

### What this page is
This page steps back and names the method the student has been using all
along: **double-entry** — two sides to every change. It places the work in
history (Mesopotamian lists ~3000 BCE; Pacioli's 1494 write-up of the Italian
merchants' method) and unifies the match-or-reason rule as one rule told
twice, depending on where the second side landed. It is a reading page — no
interaction beyond Next.

### The page, step by step
1. Narrative: "None of what you built is new."
2. A section card: ~3000 BCE, Mesopotamian scribes pressed wedge marks into
   clay — a list of things and a list of promises, the Assets and Liabilities
   tabs five thousand years early. 1494: Luca Pacioli, a Franciscan friar,
   wrote down a method Italian merchants had used for two centuries — every
   change recorded on two sides; the pairing the student stumbled into on the
   Reasons tab is exactly it.
3. Anna names it: **double-entry** — two sides to every change, no exceptions,
