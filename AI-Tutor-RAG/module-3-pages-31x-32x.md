This file is a RAG knowledge base for an AI Tutor that helps students who are lost, confused, or don't understand the directions in the interactive accounting prework. It covers seven Module 3 pages, in student order. The course teaches by DISCOVERY: the student must experience each idea before it is named, so the tutor never announces what a page will reveal. Help means orienting questions, then progressively stronger hints — mirroring the pages' own forgiving three-strike pattern (hint, stronger hint, reveal). The tutor uses "accounting identity" (never "accounting equation"), never the words "equity"/"equities" or "Net Assets", treats the repair work as an activity (not an actor — it cannot own, owe, or agree), treats documents and time periods as non-actors, and says records SHOW things while entities HAVE things. Every page has a header with a progress bar, a glossary button, and a restart button; Enter submits typed answers; Continue buttons gate advancement and unlock when the page's task completes; sorting tasks put items on the left and category zones on the right, with a "Do the rest"/"Sort the rest" control appearing after a few correct placements. A hidden triple-click dev mode exists but is INSTRUCTOR-ONLY — never point students to it.

## 31-1-welcome.html — A Season Goes By
### What this page is
This is the opening of Module 3 (Tutorial 1: Run the Season, Phase 1 of 5). It bridges from Module 2 — where the student made the repair work into a legal body, an LLC — into a whole summer of operations. The page re-displays the opening balance sheet from June 1 and asks one anchoring question that checks whether the accounting identity still holds, then narrates the summer and previews what the tutorial will discover. It ends by unlocking the workbook for the next page.

### The page, step by step
1. A narrative block reminds the student that in Module 2 the repair work became a legal body (the LLC, named from the student's earlier choice), and that on the day it became formal the books showed a single balance sheet — one snapshot. The opening balance sheet for June 1, 2026 is rendered below, with Assets totaling $4,710 and Liabilities totaling $2,250, but the "Total members' capital (Assets − Liabilities)" line is shown as "?" (not yet filled in).
2. A check card asks: "One check before the season starts — the frame from Module 1 should hold. Assets − Liabilities = $4,710 − $2,250 =". The student types a dollar amount into the input and clicks "Check" (or presses Enter). 
   - On a correct answer ($2,460) the page reveals the total and an insight box.
   - On a wrong answer, feedback reads: "Not quite — subtract Total liabilities from Total assets: $4,710 − $2,250." After three wrong attempts the page reveals anyway (the three-strike pattern).
3. The reveal (insight box): "Assets − Liabilities = Members' Capital. $4,710 − $2,250 = $2,460 — exactly the Total members' capital line. The difference you found in Module 1 now carries the LLC's name, and the balance sheet is built around the same identity — it holds by definition, every time." The balance sheet's total line now shows $2,460.
4. A second narrative block describes the summer: repairs brought money in, parts and rent got used up, a new tool was bought, some cash was taken out for yourself, and beside each change a short Reason was jotted. Then Anna — who set up the books — checks back in: "A season has gone by — let's see how the shop did."
5. A "What you'll discover" card lists three preview bullets (how the numbers look after the summer; why a single fresh snapshot leaves the main question unanswered; what a whole season of Reasons is quietly pushing toward).
6. The "Open Bike-Repair.xlsx →" Continue button is hidden until the check is resolved; clicking it advances to 31-2.

## 31-2-work-the-season.html — Read the Books
### What this page is
Phase 2 of Tutorial 1. The student opens the same three-tab workbook (Assets, Liabilities, Reasons) a full season later and, guided by Anna, drills into specific lines to discover that the flat Aug-31 numbers hide a whole summer of movement. The page is a guided walk: Anna asks a question, the student opens the named line or switches to the named tab, an insight appears, then "Next question." After six stops the page concludes that the three tabs show where things stand and why each balance moved, but never line up what the work delivered against what it used up — setting up the income statement.

### The page, step by step
1. Narrative: the workbook is shown as kept through the summer — same three tabs — with a Reason jotted beside each change. "Every line opens — click the + to see what happened behind the number." The workbook mounts with an added balance tab.
2. Anna's guided walk has six steps; each advances only when the student performs the named action (opens a line by clicking its +, or switches tabs):
   - Step 1 (Assets tab, Parts line): Anna notes Parts still reads $200 and asks whether any parts were bought or used. Open the Parts line. Insight: you bought $1,200 of parts and used $1,200 on jobs — it lands back at $200; the flat number hides a season of parts moving in then out.
   - Step 2 (Assets tab, Right of Use): $650 still, but rent was paid every month. Open it. Insight: the $650 is only the lease's last month on deposit; $1,950 of rent for June–August was paid in cash and used up — it left through the Cash line.
   - Step 3 (Assets tab, Cash): $620, down $680 from the $1,300 opened in June. Open it. Insight: $5,500 came in from repairs, but rent, parts, a new tool, paying the card off in full, and the owner's draw took out more than repairs brought in — so cash is down despite the busiest summer.
   - Step 4 (Liabilities tab, Credit Card): reads $0, was $2,030. Open it. Insight: paid off in full during the season — that cash outflow is one of the outflows seen in the Cash line.
   - Step 5 (Liabilities tab, Customer Deposit): $0, Smith's $220 gone. Open it. Insight: you built and delivered Smith's frame this summer; you no longer owe the work, so it's no longer something you owe — doing the work turned it into earnings.
   - Step 6 (switch to the Reasons tab): Anna says none of those tabs really say how the season went; switch to Reasons. Insight: one continuous list going back to the first May jot — CONTRIBUTED $2,290, WITHDRAWN −$600, GENERATED +$2,740 — but that pile has been growing since May, so it can't answer "how did the season go?" and Cash went down $680; a long list, all true, and still no answer.
3. After the sixth step, Anna summarizes: members' capital went from $2,460 in June to $4,430 now (Assets $4,430 − Liabilities $0); the story is scattered across tabs. She notes "how did the shop do?" splits into two questions ("did the work deliver more than it used up?" vs "is there more money in the bank?") that point in different directions.
4. An insight box "A workbook, not yet a statement" explains the three tabs show where things stand and why each balance moved, but never line up what the work delivered against what it used up — that comparison is next.
5. An evidence card offers four source buttons (job log, parts orders/supplier invoices, bank statements, credit card statements) so any number can be checked against its source.
6. Continue button "Next: how the season went →" advances to 32-1.
