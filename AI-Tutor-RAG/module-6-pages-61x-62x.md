### The page, step by step
The header reads "Module 6 Introduction," sub "Module 6 · ~7 min," glossary and restart buttons. Seven sections in order.

**The turn — "Same map, a different kind of entity."** Module 5 held the map against CVS, a shareholder-owned company; the whole architecture held at $250-billion scale. Module 6 holds the same map against Yale University — a nonprofit, a not-for-profit corporation that **nobody owns**. No shares, no shareholders, no dividends. If the whole right side of the balance sheet was about owners and their claims, what happens when there are no owners? The map still applies — A − L still computes a difference, the difference still needs explaining — but the names change, the difference gets classified a different way, and the nonprofit world adds its own real complications.

**What CVS confirmed — "Six claims that survived contact with a real company."** (1) The identity holds at any scale: $253,538 − $178,156 = $75,382 (2025) and $253,215 − $177,485 = $75,730 (2024). (2) Cash gets its own explanation — read the labels: the BS cash line fell $133M ($8,586 → $8,453) while the CFS reconciled its broader scope down $172M ($8,884 → $8,712). (3) Equity changes only through the statements: down $348M in a profitable year, traced +$769 / −$1,115 / −$2. (4) The income statement explains the generated part: $1,768M = $1,728M + $40M; $1,768 + $526 = $2,294M comprehensive attributable. (5) The whole architecture is one connected system. (6) Restrictive recognition still draws its line — CVS's brand is nowhere on CVS's balance sheet.

**What changes when nobody owns the place.** Eight numbered points, and this section is the module's vocabulary introduction (new terms bold at first use): (1) the identity is untouched — Yale: $59,043 − $9,672 = $49,371, and $55,315 − $9,278 = $46,037 the year before. (2) The difference gets a new name, for a real reason — **Net Assets**; a change of name, not of nature: still that definitional difference, not a pool of cash. (3) Net Assets is classified **by donor restriction, not by source** — Without Donor Restrictions ($10,762M) and With Donor Restrictions ($38,609M, about 78%). (4) Only donors create binding restrictions — a board designation is intent, stays Without. (5) The statements are renamed, and there are **two** explanatory statements, not three: the **Statement of Financial Position**; the **Statement of Activities** (whose bottom line is **change in net assets**) doing the combined work of an income statement and a statement of changes in equity; and the Statement of Cash Flows. (6) Restrictions **release** — $1,615M reclassified With → Without in FY2025; no new resources; total unchanged. (7) The endowment is the elephant in the room — $49,708M of investments dominating the asset side. (8) A total is not a spending account — the liquidity footnote reports about $5,029M available within one year against $59 billion of assets.

**The bridge exercise — "The map, revisited for a nonprofit."** A link opens 52-1-The-Statement-Map.html ("Open the statement map →"), and ten expandable items each reveal a three-entity comparison — bike shop / CVS / Yale — for one map element: the entity; Architecture: A − L = ?; what the difference's split tracks (source vs donor-restriction status); balance sheet; statement of changes; income statement; bottom line; cash flow statement; the unique move (releases — CVS and the shop had none); restrictive recognition (owner's skill and customers / the CVS brand / Yale's collections, human capital, and name).

**The Yale preview — "Where we are going."** Yale's FY2025 report is the map made real for a nonprofit: the SFP ($59,043 / $9,672 / $49,371, split $10,762 without and $38,609 with) and a hard look at what the statement does NOT show — the collections (the Beinecke, the Art Gallery, the Peabody) at billions in fair value and zero on the books under ASC 958-360, not expensed, not recorded anywhere; the Yale name and the faculty off the books for the same reason CVS's brand is. The cash question again with a nonprofit twist (−$293M vs −$380M — read the labels). The change-in-net-assets question ($3,334M up — earned, given, or merely released?). Two statements, not three. The promise-of-the-module insight: examples, not the whole list; the map survives contact with reality; the details are where the expertise lives.

**"Why the nonprofit label fits" — the expandable "A note on the name."** Module 5 warned that "equity" promises more than the number can deliver; a nonprofit makes the point unavoidable — Yale has no owners, so the profession does not call the difference "equity" at all; it calls it Net Assets. The plainer words shouldn't fool anyone either: "Net Assets" can sound like a spendable amount; it is the same definitional difference — at Yale $49.4 billion, mostly restricted by donors or tied up in the endowment and the buildings. What the name buys is a better fit to the accountability structure: a company's difference is split by who put money in versus what operations generated; a nonprofit's by whether donors restricted the money and whether those restrictions have been met. Same difference; different question asked of it. Read "Net Assets" as a classification of a computed difference, never as a pot of money.

**The learning objectives.** M6-LO1 identify the accounting entity in Yale's consolidated statements (same map, no owners); M6-LO2 locate Yale's three statements, map each onto the architecture, and explain why a nonprofit has two explanatory statements where CVS had three; M6-LO3 explain why a nonprofit calls the difference Net Assets, how it is classified with/without donor restrictions, and why board designations remain Without; M6-LO4 explain releases as a reclassification leaving total net assets unchanged; M6-LO5 (awareness) how nonprofit conventions create complications — endowment spending allocation, functional classification of expenses, the collections election (mirrors M5-LO5); M6-LO6 distinguish net-asset classification from liquidity and use the required footnote; M6-LO7 apply restrictive recognition to Yale (collections, human capital, the name).

Nav: "Back" → 53-6-The-Full-Architecture.html; "A Nonprofit Statement Map →" → 62-1-A-Nonprofit-Statement-Map.html.

### If the student is lost
- "If nobody owns Yale, whose money is it?" Orient: what does the identity compute, regardless of owners? Nudge: the difference between recorded assets and recorded liabilities exists by arithmetic; the question is only what to call it and how to classify it. Stronger hint: with no owners there is no ownership claim to classify by — so the split tracks donor restrictions instead of contributed-versus-generated; that is points 2 and 3.
- "What's the difference between a restriction and a board designation?" Orient: who imposed each one, and who can undo it? Nudge: a donor's restriction binds; the board's designation is its own intent. Stronger hint: the board can change its mind at any meeting, so board-designated amounts stay in Without Donor Restrictions — only donors can bind (point 4, and the glossary).
- "Why only two explanatory statements?" Orient: which two jobs did CVS's income statement and statement of shareholders' equity do? Nudge: for a nonprofit, every change in net assets flows through one statement. Stronger hint: the Statement of Activities does both jobs — its bottom line is change in net assets, not net income (point 5).
- "Is $49 billion of net assets money Yale can spend?" Orient: what does the note say the number is? Nudge: a classification of a computed difference, not a bank balance. Stronger hint: point 8 — the liquidity footnote's ~$5,029M available within one year, against $59B of assets, answers the spending question; the walk returns to this on 63-5.
- Tutor conduct: this page INTRODUCES the NFP vocabulary — after it, use it freely on 6x pages. Never frame the collections exclusion as inability; the page says deliberate convention, and 63-1 stages it fully. Keep the three-entity bridge handy — it is the fastest way to re-anchor a lost student.
