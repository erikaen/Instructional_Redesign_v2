# Module 3 — Tutorial 3 (Depreciation), pages 33-x

This file documents four pages of Tutorial 3 in Module 3. The course teaches by
discovery: the student experiences each idea before it is named, so the tutor
orients with questions and follows the pages' own forgiving hint → stronger hint →
reveal pacing. The tutor never announces what a page is about to reveal. Help
means orienting questions first, then progressively stronger hints; the tutor
confirms an answer only after a genuine attempt.

Conduct reminders encoded below (the tutor mirrors these and never blurts them
ahead of the page): the Module-1 discovery that personal money quietly covered
repair-work costs (found at the negative cash tally); any name for the
Assets-minus-Liabilities difference before the page gives it (it is only "the
difference" until a page names it); the Module-3 depreciation discovery on the
Reasons tab (the depreciation amounts are GIVEN, never calculated — no
useful-life math in this course); and the fact that revenue and expenses are a
SORTING of the Reasons tab's GENERATED rows (no Revenue/Expenses source tabs
exist; "closing the books" is never mentioned in this course). Language the tutor
mirrors: "accounting identity" (never the phrase with the word for the balance
formula); the repair work is an activity, not an actor (it cannot own, owe, or
agree); documents and time periods are not actors; records SHOW things, entities
HAVE things. Every page has a header with a progress bar, a glossary button, and
a restart button (except the final recap, which has only glossary); Enter submits
typed answers; Continue buttons gate advancement and unlock when the page's task
completes. A hidden triple-click dev mode is instructor-only — never point
students to it.

## 33-1-Two-Kinds-of-Used-Up.html — Two Kinds of Used-Up

## 33-2-The-Third-Kind.html — The Third Kind
### What this page is
Phase 2 of 4. The wear is real used-up but no row names it, and before a row can
exist someone must put a number on it. The page asks how the season could put a
number on the tools' wear and offers four options; the student must reject
watch-it (like parts), let-time-lapse (like rent), and look-it-up (eBay market)
before selecting that none of those works and someone must choose a rule. That
choice leads Anna to name depreciation as an allocation — a chosen convention,
not a reading — and the student clicks "Record the wear" so two Depreciation
rows land on the Reasons tab and the Income statement completes. This is the
page that names depreciation, and the discovery happens by ruling out the other
methods first.
### The page, step by step
- **Header / phase title.** Phase 2 of 4. Phase title "The Third Kind".
  Narrative: the wear is real used-up, no row names it yet; before a row can
  exist, someone has to put a number on it — where would one come from?
- **MCQ card.** "How could the season put a number on the tools' wear?" with
  four options (click to select; Submit is disabled until a selection):
  - **A.** "Watch it, like parts — count what left the shelf." (Wrong. Feedback:
    there's nothing to watch; nothing left the shelf; the stand is right there,
    whole, doing its job.)
  - **B.** "Let time lapse it, like rent — one season on the calendar, one share
    gone." (Wrong. Feedback: closer — time is part of it, but rent came priced by
    the month and the calendar did the whole job; the tools weren't bought by
    the season, and a stand used on 21 jobs isn't the same as one that sat; time
    alone doesn't produce the number.)
  - **C.** "Look it up — find what a season-old stand fetches on eBay, and charge
    the drop." (Wrong. Feedback: the market would give a number, but keeping
    that up for every tool every season isn't worth the effort, and it's the
    wrong question — what the tools would fetch today, not how to spread what
    you paid across the seasons they serve; there's no true wear-cost sitting
    out there to look up.)
  - **D.** "None of those works — someone has to choose a rule." (Correct.
    Feedback: right — nothing to watch go, no month that expires, and market
    prices answer a different question; if the season is to carry a share of the
    tools' cost, the share has to come from a chosen rule.)
- A wrong submit shows feedback plus a Reset. The correct submit plays success
  and Anna's dialog appears.
- **Anna (before recording).** She explains accountants chose a rule: spread the
  thing's cost over the seasons of its useful life and charge each season its
  share — the simplest rule splits the cost evenly, the same share every
  season, straight down the line. The share is called depreciation. Be clear
  about what it is: an allocation — a chosen convention, not observed use, not
  the mere passage of time, not a market price looked up; a rule standing in for
  a mix of time, wear and tear, and judgment. For the shop, each share is just
  cost ÷ useful life: $200 this season on the tools, equipment & fixtures, and
  $30 on the laptop — two separate lines so each can be argued with on its own.
  The useful lives and the schedule behind those numbers follow generally
  accepted accounting principles (a course of its own); take $200 and $30 as
  given, the same way the original purchase prices were taken as given. Continue
  button: "Record the wear →".
- **Record the wear.** Clicking Continue updates the workbook with depreciation
  and switches to the Reasons tab, where two Depreciation rows appear at the
  bottom of the GENERATED rows ($200 tools/equipment/fixtures, $30 laptop). Anna
  (recorded state) explains: the third kind of used-up is on the tab at last;
  flip to Income — Parts $1,200 · Rent $1,950 · Depreciation $200 + $30 →
  Expenses $3,380, the placeholder gone; flip to Assets — the Tools & Repair
  Equipment line carries −$160, Fixtures −$40, the Laptop −$30 (the wear didn't
  just cost the season, it lowered what the records carry). An insight box "An
  allocation, openly chosen" notes the $200 isn't a reading of anything — it's
  the season's share of a cost, set by a rule someone chose (and taking none
  back in May was also a choice). A Continue button "What the wear did to the
  books →" navigates to 33-3.
### If the student is lost
- **"Which option do I pick?"** Orient: the last page showed two methods that
  worked for parts and rent; do either work for the tools? Nudge: re-read the
  feedback for A and B — parts need something to watch leave, rent needs a
  month that expires; do the tools do either? Stronger hint: nothing leaves
  the shelf, no month expires, and a market price answers a different question —
  so none of the first three works; pick the option that says someone must
  choose a rule.
- **"Why is 'look it up' wrong — eBay has prices?"** Orient: what question would
  the eBay price answer? Nudge: it tells you what the tool would fetch today,
  not how to spread what you paid across the seasons it serves. Stronger hint:
  the page's feedback says the market gives a number but it's the wrong question
  and not worth the effort; the number that lands here comes from a rule, not a
  market.
- **"Do I have to compute useful life?"** Orient: the page says take $200 and
  $30 as given — the same way the purchase prices were taken as given. Nudge:
  no useful-life math in this course. Stronger hint: depreciation here is a
  chosen rule; the specific lives and schedule follow GAAP (out of scope); the
  student takes the given shares.
- **After recording, "what just changed?"** Orient: check the Reasons tab (two
  new Depreciation rows at the bottom of GENERATED) and the Income tab
  (placeholder gone, Expenses $3,380). The tutor can point to the tabs.
### TUTOR-ONLY answer key
- **Correct choice: D — "None of those works — someone has to choose a rule."**
- The page is a single MCQ (no third-strike auto-reveal); wrong answers give
  feedback and a Reset.
- Depreciation = an allocation: a season's share of a long-lived thing's cost by
  a chosen rule (in the simplest rule, cost ÷ useful life, split evenly). It is
  not observed use, not the mere passage of time, and not a market price looked
  up — a convention standing in for time, wear and tear, and judgment.
- **Given amounts (never calculated by the student):** $200 for the season on
  tools, equipment & fixtures; $30 for the season on the laptop. Two separate
  lines so each can be argued with on its own.
- After "Record the wear": the Reasons tab gets two Depreciation rows ($200 and
  $30) at the bottom of the GENERATED rows; the Income statement completes —
  Parts $1,200 + Rent $1,950 + Depreciation $200 + $30 = Expenses $3,380, the
  placeholder gone; Assets show Tools & Repair Equipment −$160, Fixtures −$40,
  Laptop −$30.
- The tutor guides toward "a chosen rule" without blurting "depreciation" before
  the student picks D and Anna names it; confirm only after a genuine attempt.
### Anchor
anchor: the last line is still a placeholder
### Before and after

books and to finish the bottom line.

## 33-3-The-Bottom-Line.html — The Bottom Line
### What this page is
Phase 3 of 4. With depreciation recorded, the page looks at what the wear did to
the other side of the books (Assets), names carrying value (book value) — cost
minus the depreciation charged so far, a path the records walk, not a market
price — and then completes the income statement to reveal Net income $2,640. It
also plants an open thread: a $2,640 season yet the Cash line reads $620, down
from $1,300 on opening day — profitable, and poorer in cash; both true. This is
the page that finishes the bottom line and sets up the next module's question.
### The page, step by step
- **Header / phase title.** Phase 3 of 4. Phase title "The Bottom Line".
  Narrative: the Expenses tab is complete; before the bottom line, one look at
  what the wear did to the other side of the books.
- **Workbook opens on Assets, Laptop line expanded.** The workbook mounts with
  depreciation recorded and opens on the Assets tab with the Laptop line
  expanded.
- **Step 0 (Anna).** Look at the Laptop line: cost $610, this season's wear
  −$30, the records carry it at $580 now. That number has a name — carrying
  value, also called book value: what's left of the cost after the depreciation
  charged so far. Be clear about what it is — a cost walking down a chosen path;
  the market (what a comparable laptop fetches on eBay tonight) is deliberately
  not chased, because chasing that price every season isn't worth the effort and
  isn't what depreciation is; the cost walks its chosen path while the market
  answers its own question, so don't expect book and market to agree. An insight
  box "Carrying value is a path, not a price" adds: cost minus the depreciation
  charged so far tells where the number has been, not what the laptop would
  fetch; and unlike rent — where a lapsed month is simply gone — a fully
  depreciated tool is still on the bench and may still be worth something; the
  path runs out, the thing doesn't. Continue button: "Finish the income
  statement →".
- **Step 1 — the bottom line.** Clicking Continue adds the income statement tabs
  and switches the workbook to the Income tab. Anna announces the bottom line at
  last: Net income $2,640. The shop delivered $6,020 of work this season and
  used up $3,380 getting it done — parts, months of space, and wear; every kind
  of used-up counted, nothing left open. An insight box "How the season went —
  answered" states: one statement read straight off the sorted season rows (the
  working sheet's two bands): Revenue $6,020 − Expenses $3,380 = Net income
  $2,640, the answer the jumbled pile couldn't give. Then a "next-phase" message
  raises one loose thread: a $2,640 season and the Cash line reads $620, down from
  $1,300 on opening day — profitable, and poorer in cash; both true; the thread
  stays open past the end of this module. A Continue button "Finish Module 3 →"
  navigates to 33-4.
### If the student is lost
- **"What is carrying value / book value?"** Orient: look at the Laptop line —
  cost $610, wear −$30; what number do the records show now? Nudge: it's cost
  minus the depreciation charged so far. Stronger hint: it's a path the records
  walk down by rule, not a price the market sets; don't expect it to match what
  the laptop would fetch.
- **"Why doesn't book value match the market?"** Orient: what question would the
  market answer, and what question do the records answer? Nudge: the records
  deliberately don't chase the market price every season. Stronger hint: the
  page says the cost walks its chosen path while the market answers its own
  question — book and market need not agree.
- **"So a fully depreciated tool is worth $0?"** Orient: what does "the path runs
  out, the thing doesn't" mean? Nudge: rent's lapsed month is gone; is a worn
  tool gone? Stronger hint: a fully depreciated tool can still be on the bench
  and may still be worth something — the path runs out, the thing doesn't.
- **"How is Net income figured?"** Orient: read the two bands of the statement.
  Nudge: Revenue minus Expenses. Stronger hint: $6,020 − $3,380 = $2,640, read
  straight off the sorted season rows.
- **"We made $2,640 but cash went down?"** Orient: that's the loose thread the
  page names. Nudge: profit and cash are different questions; the page says both
  are true and leaves it open. Stronger hint: don't try to resolve it here —
  it's the next module's question; just hold the thread.
### TUTOR-ONLY answer key
- **Carrying value (book value)** = cost minus the depreciation charged so far.
  For the Laptop: $610 − $30 = $580. It is a path the records walk, not a
  price; book and market need not agree; a fully depreciated tool can still be
  on the bench and worth something.
- **Net income** = Revenue $6,020 − Expenses $3,380 = $2,640. Expenses = Parts
  $1,200 + Rent $1,950 + Depreciation $200 + $30.
- This page has no MCQ and no third-strike reveal; it is a two-step reveal
  (carrying value, then the bottom line) gated by Continue buttons.
- **Open thread the page plants:** Cash is $620, down from $1,300 on opening
  day, despite a $2,640 net income — profitable and poorer in cash; both true;
  intentionally left open past this module.
- The tutor reveals these only as the page does; confirm the numbers after the
  student has looked at the lines.
### Anchor
anchor: a fully depreciated tool is still on the bench, and may still be worth something.
### Before and after
Before — 33-2 recorded depreciation ($200 + $30) and completed the expense
