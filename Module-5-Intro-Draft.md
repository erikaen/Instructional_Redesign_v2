## The turn (near-student voice)

The bike shop's story is over. You built its records from a shoebox of
receipts, drew its boundary, kept its Reasons, and ended holding a complete
set of financial statements — with an investor's offer on the table that the
statements alone could not price.

The way you learned is about to change too. Modules 1 through 4 never told
you where they were going: you did the work first, felt the problem, and the
names came last. That was deliberate, and it ends here. From this module on,
the course follows the standard pattern you will meet in every accounting
course after this one: **learning objectives first** — you are told what you
are expected to be able to do; **then the material**; **then assessments**
of whether you can do it.

Module 5 has one job: take the map you built in the bike shop and hold it up
against the financial statements of a real, complex entity — **CVS Health**.
The map applies. The complications are real. Both facts matter.

## What the bike shop taught (near-student voice — the eight points)

1. **The entity comes first.** Financial statements are *about* something,
   and knowing exactly what they are about is the first act of reading them.
   In the bike shop you drew that boundary yourself — before any legal form
   existed — and the accounting entity and the legal entity turned out to be
   different ideas that only later coincided.

2. **The architecture of the balance sheet: Assets, Liabilities, and the
   Reasons.** In a for-profit corporation like CVS Health, the Reasons side
   carries the label **Equities**.
   1. Assets and liabilities are abstract concepts: an asset is a present
      right to an economic benefit; a liability is a present obligation to
      sacrifice economic benefits in the future.
   2. The benefits may be tied to tangible things — the tools, the fixtures —
      or to intangible things: the right to collect cash from a customer
      (Ridgeline's $300), the right to occupy space (the $650 Right of Use).
      The record does not care whether you can drop it on your foot; it cares
      about the right and the obligation.

3. **The equities accounts are creatures of the record.** Two things about
   them matter most:
   1. They pertain *only* to the assets and liabilities that have been
      entered into the formal accounting record. Nothing else, however real,
      reaches them.
   2. They can be read as classifying every change in net recorded assets by
      its source: **contributed** by owners, or **generated** through the
      operations of the entity. The bike shop's two piles — Contributed and
      Generated — are exactly this classification, kept from the first jot.

4. **The statement of changes in shareholders' equity** — the corporate
   analogue of the bike shop's Statement of Member's Capital — provides the
   detail on how each equities account changed over the period. The shop's
   walk from $2,460 to $4,500 (Contributed $2,290 unchanged; Generated $170
   plus net income $2,640; Withdrawn $600) is the same statement CVS
   publishes, with more accounts and more kinds of change.

5. **The income statement** provides the detail on how the *generated* part
   of the change in net assets came about. It shows what increased net
   assets (**Revenues**) and what decreased them (**Expenses**). Some items,
   by convention, are shown only net — those appear as **Gains** (net
   increases) or **Losses** (net decreases). The bike shop's income
   statement had no gains or losses — every line was gross — which is itself
   worth noticing when you meet statements that do.
   1. Because assets and liabilities are abstract benefits and obligations,
      changes in net assets are not always matched by cash moving in or out.
      The shop earned $2,640 while its cash fell $680 — the same shop, on
      two honest clocks.

6. **The cash flow statement** provides the detail on the change in one
   particular asset — cash. It is broken into cash flows relating to
   **operating**, **investing**, and **financing** activities. The shop's
   $2,350, $(400), $(2,630) walked its cash from $1,300 to $620 with nothing
   left unexplained.

7. **Real entities add complicating detail; the architecture holds.** The
   financial statements of actual, complex entities carry complications the
   bike shop never met — but the big picture is exactly as described above.

8. **Probably the single most important thing: restrictive recognition.**
   Information that is clearly relevant to the value of the entity is not
   necessarily recognized in the formal accounts. Recognition draws a
   restrictive line: items enter the record only when the conventions'
   evidence test is met. The owner's skill at the bench and the loyal
   customer base were the bike shop's examples — real enough to price
   (Freddie offered $20,000 for 30% of a business whose recorded capital was
   $4,500), invisible in the accounts. The gap between the records and the
   offer is not an error in either number; the two answer different
   questions.

## The bridge exercise: the map, revisited

Before CVS, the student reviews the bike shop's statement map (the
three-column map: two balance sheets flanking the statements that explain
the changes between them) and finds where each of the eight points shows up
concretely. Working key:

| # | Point | Where it manifests in the bike shop's map |
|---|-------|-------------------------------------------|
| 1 | Entity | The statements are Bike-Repair.xlsx's — the boundary drawn in 1.1, given legal form in Module 2; the records never mixed in the day job or the personal card's groceries |
| 2 | Architecture | The tabs themselves: Assets, Liabilities, Reasons → Balance Sheet; intangibles sit beside tools (A/R $300, Right of Use $650) |
| 3 | Equities = record-only, two sources | Member's Capital $4,500 = exactly recorded A − L; its piles are Contributed $2,290 / Generated $2,810 / Withdrawn $(600) |
| 4 | Statement of changes | Statement of Member's Capital: $2,460 → $4,500, pile by pile |
| 5 | Income statement | Revenue $6,020 − Expenses $3,380 = $2,640, feeding Generated; no gains/losses — all gross (noticing the absence is the point); NI $2,640 vs cash $(680) |
| 6 | Cash flow statement | Operating $2,350 / Investing $(400) / Financing $(2,630); $1,300 → $620 |
| 7 | Complications absent | One legal entity, wholly owned, one net income, cash is a bank account — every simplification about to be relaxed |
| 8 | Restrictive recognition | The $20,000-for-30% offer vs $4,500 recorded capital; skill and customer base in neither ledger |

## Where we are going: CVS Health (near-student voice)

CVS Health is a complex accounting entity — a consolidation of many
different legal entities presented as if they were one. The map drawn in the
bike shop applies to it directly: the same architecture, the same explaining
statements, the same restrictive recognition. And applying the map to a real
entity involves complicated details. Among the high-level complications
experts in analyzing (or producing) financial statements must contend with:

- **What, exactly, is labeled Cash.** It will be very important to
  understand precisely what the statements call Cash — the line is not the
  bike shop's single bank account.
- **Less-than-100% ownership.** When the accounting entity does not own all
  of every legal entity folded into its statements, the statements must show
  the effects — the interest that belongs to others (historically called
  minority interest; labeled *noncontrolling interests* in current
  statements).
- **Two net incomes.** The conventions produce two net income calculations,
  one more comprehensive than the other — and the statements carry both.

These are examples, not the whole list. The promise of the module: the map
survives contact with reality; the details are where the expertise lives.

## Draft learning objectives (for Rick's edit — the new pattern applied)

By the end of Module 5, the student will be able to:

- **M5-LO1:** Identify the accounting entity in CVS Health's financial
  statements and explain how a consolidated entity differs from a single
  legal entity.
- **M5-LO2:** Locate the four statements in a real 10-K and map each onto
  the architecture built in the bike shop (balance sheet; changes in
  shareholders' equity; income statement; cash flows).
- **M5-LO3:** State precisely what CVS's statements label as Cash and why
  the definition matters to the cash flow statement.
- **M5-LO4:** Explain what noncontrolling interests represent and where
  their effects appear.
- **M5-LO5:** Distinguish net income from comprehensive income and describe
  the relationship between the two.
- **M5-LO6:** Apply restrictive recognition to a real entity: give examples
  of things relevant to CVS's value that its statements do not recognize.
