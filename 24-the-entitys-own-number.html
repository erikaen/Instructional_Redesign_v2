Activity 2.4: The Entity’s Own Number

Module 2 · Getting Formal · ~10 min

The LLC exists, it has its rules, and it owns the business. But to the outside world it still has no identity of its own. You go to open a business bank account — and the bank stops you:

THE BANK
“We can’t open this in your name. We need the LLC’s number — its EIN — not your Social Security number.”
Until now, the repair work has run through your personal accounts — your personal checking (Elm City Bank, $3,274) and your personal credit card (Harborline Visa) — both co-mingled: business deposits sitting next to your paycheck, parts charges next to your groceries. You’ve sorted them apart by hand every month since 1.1. The entity needs its own.
What You’ll Work Out
  • How the entity gets an identity the outside world will deal with.
  • What finally ends the by-hand sorting you’ve done since day one.
  • The one move here that actually changes your balance sheet.

With the EIN in hand, you can finally give the business its own accounts — and pull it out of your personal ones. Do it in three moves.

The split isn’t just a story — here’s the paper. Four statements close out the month: the entity’s own accounts, and your personal ones now that the business is out of them.

The entity stands on its own
What You Worked Out
  • The LLC has its own EIN — an identity to the outside world, separate from your SSN.
  • It has its own bank account and its own card; the business no longer rides your personal accounts.
  • Co-mingling is over — the by-hand sorting you’ve done since 1.1 is now institutional.
  • The only move that touched the books was your $500: a real capital contribution (Cash 0 → 500; members’ capital 1,790 → 2,290).
The deeper point

The entity is now a counterparty in its own right — its own number, its own accounts, able to owe and be owed in its own name. The boundary you first drew by hand in 1.1 is now backed by banks and the IRS. None of that was a legal-form trick: the only thing that moved the economics was your own decision to put in $500.
