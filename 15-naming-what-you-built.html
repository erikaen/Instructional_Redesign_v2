Five sessions in, your workbook holds everything you figured out at the workbench: an Assets list (what you have), a Liabilities list (what you owe), and the Reasons your net moved — sorted into the two kinds you found, what you put in and what the work made.

You’ve been calling them by your own words. Today you put them on one page, watch how they fit together — and the names you’ve been circling finally arrive.

ANNA
“You built the whole thing from scratch — no textbook, just the workbench and a spreadsheet. Turns out it has names. Old ones. Let’s lay it out and earn them.”
What You’ll Discover
  • Your net (have − owe) and your Reasons total to the same number — and always will, by construction.
  • That gives you the formal names: the identity, the balance sheet, the residual.
  • And the boundary you drew back in session one had a name all along.

Everything in one workbook for the first time. Notice Cash sits on the Assets list now — back in session three it was just a flow you couldn’t pin down. Now that you’ve split the repair work’s money from your personal money, the repair work’s cash ($0 on hand) is one of its assets.

ANNA
“Two totals matter now. The records show Assets of $3,410 and Liabilities of $1,450. The gap between them is your net. What is it?”
Your net = Assets − Liabilities = $3,410 − $1,450 = ?

Go back to the very first session. Before any of this, you did one quiet thing: you drew a line. This stand, this card, this slice of the bank account — part of the repair work. Your groceries, your gas, your paycheck — not. You kept asking the same question of every item: is this the repair work, or is this me?

ANNA
“That line you drew has a name. The subject of a set of books — the thing the Assets and Liabilities belong to — is the entity. And here’s the part people miss: you didn’t find it. You made it, by deciding what’s in and what’s out. The entity is a perspective you adopt for a purpose, not an object sitting in the world.”
The boundary is the work; the name follows

An entity is anything you can draw a clear edge around for a reason — this repair work, a person, a school, a food bank. It isn’t the bicycle industry (too many entities at once), or Smith’s deposit (that’s something that happens to an entity), or “last May” (a slice of time). Draw the line in the wrong place and every number after it is answering the wrong question.

One quick widening before you stop. Your repair work has an owner — you. What about an entity with no owner?

ANNA
“Plenty of real entities have nobody to call an owner — a food bank, a museum, a public university, a church. Real books, real Assets and Liabilities, same identity: A − L = the residual. But the for-profit label Equities grew up where owners exist. With no owner, the convention writes Net Assets instead. Same arithmetic — the residual is still just recorded Assets minus recorded Liabilities. The name changes because the accountability does: your shop answers to you; a food bank answers to its mission and its donors.”

Same identity, different label

Net Assets is the same kind of definitional residual as Equities — not a pool of cash, and not anyone’s ownership stake. A no-owner entity further splits its Net Assets by whether donors attached strings (with vs. without donor restrictions) — but that machinery, and how to read it, is a Module 3 story. For now: same identity, different label, because the accountability differs.

Step back from the workbench for a second. None of what you built is new.

None of this is new
  • ~3000 BCE. Scribes in Mesopotamia pressed wedge marks into clay: so much grain in the storehouse, so many sheep, who owes what at harvest. A list of things and a list of promises — your Assets and Liabilities tabs, five thousand years early.
  • 1494. A Franciscan friar, Luca Pacioli, writes down a method Italian merchants had used for two centuries: every change gets recorded on two sides. The pairing you stumbled into in your Reasons tab — where each change had a partner — is exactly it.
ANNA
“That pairing has a name: double-entry. Two sides to every change. It’s how the pairs canceled and the net-movers stood out when you pruned your Reasons. And it’s not a relic — every number on every financial statement filed today still gets there by double-entry. The identity and the balance sheet you built by hand are the same structures the largest companies in the world file.”
Tutorial 1.5 complete — and Module 1 with it
The names you earned
  • You drew a boundary for a purpose — that’s the entity. You made it; you didn’t find it.
  • Recorded Assets minus recorded Liabilities is Equities — a definitional residual, not a pile of cash, and not your net worth: the records leave real things out.
  • A − L = Equities is the accounting identity — it holds by construction, and the page that lays it out is the balance sheet.
  • The residual grows two ways — contributed and generated — and a no-owner entity calls it Net Assets.
  • Every change has two sides: double-entry, how every number on every statement gets there.
You invented the system. Now you have its names

Identify the entity. List its Assets and Liabilities. The residual is Equities (owned) or Net Assets (no owner). A − L = E, by construction — the accounting identity, on a balance sheet, maintained by double-entry. Pacioli wrote it down in 1494. You worked it out at the workbench in May.
