# Module learning objectives

## Module 1

Module 1 builds the entire accounting picture from scratch: the student draws the entity boundary by sorting things, prices the Things list, separates cash into Assets and Liabilities, discovers the match-or-reason rule and Contributed/Generated, and arrives at the accounting identity, the balance sheet, and double-entry. The arc goes from a hands-on sort to the formal names for what the student already built by hand.

- M1-LO1: Students will be able to distinguish what belongs inside the repair-work entity from what stays outside it, explaining why the line is about membership in the work rather than location, use, or ownership.
- M1-LO2: Students will be able to assign a dollar amount to each item on the Things list and identify the traceable source behind each value (receipt, eBay comp, shelf count), recognizing that different observers see different slices of the same list.
- M1-LO3: Students will be able to separate repair-related rows from personal rows on the bank and credit-card statements, and explain why net cash flow alone cannot tell whether a month was good or bad.
- M1-LO4: Students will be able to classify money coming in as either theirs to keep or as creating an obligation, and distinguish an upfront payment (Smith) that creates a promise from a mere conversation (Jones) that does not.
- M1-LO5: Students will be able to distinguish money that is gone from money that bought a present right to use something later (prepaid rent), and construct the Assets and Liabilities lists from the Cash, Things, and Promises sheets.
- M1-LO6: Students will be able to apply the match-or-reason rule — every change in an Asset or a Liability gets either a match (the other line of the same transaction) or a reason on the Reasons tab — and explain why matched pairs never move the difference between Assets and Liabilities.
- M1-LO7: Students will be able to classify the reasons that move the difference into Contributed (funded from outside) and Generated (produced by the work), tracing the $160 personal shortfall as a Contributed reason.
- M1-LO8: Students will be able to compute the difference (Assets − Liabilities), recognize it as the accounting identity that holds by construction, lay it out as a balance sheet, explain why the records show less than there is (the difference is a gap between two recorded lists, not a net-worth figure), and describe double-entry as every change recorded on two sides — a match and a reason.

## Module 2

Module 2 formalizes the entity: the student feels the gap between the accounting boundary (informal, in the books) and the legal boundary, files a Certificate of Organization to create an LLC, drafts an Operating Agreement and contributes the business at book carryover, then gives the entity its own EIN, bank account, and card — completing Schedule A and signing. The keystone is that the accounting entity came first and the legal body is a wrapper around it.

- M2-LO1: Students will be able to explain that only the books see the bike-repair role as separate while the bank, credit-card company, customer, and law see one undivided you, and distinguish the accounting boundary (informal, in the books) from the legal boundary (the LLC).
- M2-LO2: Students will be able to describe how a Certificate of Organization brings a legal entity into existence — a "legal person" that can own things, owe debts, sign contracts, and be sued in its own name — and explain why the filing changes legal status but not the balance sheet.
- M2-LO3: Students will be able to explain why the Operating Agreement stays a draft until Schedule A is complete (the contributing isn't finished), and describe the contribution of the whole business at book carryover — not one number changes, only "the difference" becomes Members' Capital.
- M2-LO4: Students will be able to describe limited liability as a legal fact that changes who can be pursued, not what the records show, and explain the keystone: accounting-entity ≠ legal-entity (the accounting entity came first; the LLC is a legal wrapper).
- M2-LO5: Students will be able to explain how an EIN, an LLC bank account, and an LLC card give the entity its own identity and end co-mingling, and identify the $1,300 capital contribution as the only books-changing move of the module (Cash 0→1,300; members' capital 990→2,290).
- M2-LO6: Students will be able to complete Schedule A line for line from the Company's books and explain why it agrees exactly with the opening balance sheet, and describe the two signatures (once for the Company, once as the Sole Member) as the two roles made legal and literal.

## Module 3

Module 3 runs the shop through its first season and builds the income statement: the student reads a flat balance-sheet snapshot and discovers the Reasons pile can't answer "how did the season go?", sorts reasons into DELIVERED and USED UP (revenue follows the work, not the cash), names Revenue and Expenses as the two temporary accounts, and handles depreciation — the third kind of used-up — to reach Net income and carrying value.

- M3-LO1: Students will be able to read a flat balance-sheet snapshot and trace the season of movement it hides (parts bought and used, rent used, cash in and out, the card paid off, the customer deposit cleared).
- M3-LO2: Students will be able to explain why the Reasons pile (a continuous, jumbled, never-resetting list) cannot answer "how did the season go?" and why that question requires a period-scoped tally.
- M3-LO3: Students will be able to sort the season's reasons into DELIVERED and USED UP piles, total each, and read their difference as the answer to how the season went.
- M3-LO4: Students will be able to explain that revenue follows the work, not the cash — a delivery counts when the work is done (Ridgeline: work now, cash later; Smith: cash earlier, work now) — and trace the Accounts Receivable line that appears when cash has not yet arrived.
- M3-LO5: Students will be able to name Revenue and Expenses as the two temporary (period-scoped) accounts, lay out the income statement (Revenue minus Expenses = Net income), and explain how the income statement explains a balance-sheet move (Net income feeds GENERATED, never CONTRIBUTED; the draw is WITHDRAWN).
- M3-LO6: Students will be able to distinguish three kinds of used-up — parts (directly observed), rent (time-lapsed), and wear on tools/laptop (neither) — and explain why depreciation is an allocation by an openly chosen convention rather than a reading.
- M3-LO7: Students will be able to define carrying value as a path the records walk (cost minus depreciation charged so far, not a market price), complete the income statement (Revenue $6,020 − Expenses $3,380 = Net income $2,640), and explain why book and market need not agree.

## Module 4

Module 4 closes the books and connects the statements: the student explains why the owner's labor has no expense line (a draw is not an expense), builds the capital bridge and closes the period, constructs the Statement of Cash Flows from three buckets, reconciles net income to operating cash (two honest clocks), and discovers articulation — the statements tie out because the difference travels through them in full.

- M4-LO1: Students will be able to identify that the owner's bench labor has no expense line (a draw is not an expense and never changes net income), and distinguish the two hats (employee at the bench vs. owner on the Operating Agreement) as the records treat them differently.
- M4-LO2: Students will be able to construct the capital bridge (opening Members' Capital + net income − draw = closing Members' Capital) and identify that Contributed sat still while the whole swing ran through Generated.
- M4-LO3: Students will be able to describe closing as filing a finished period (Revenue and Expenses are archived as a single row and the tabs reset to $0), and explain that "temporary" means period-scoped, not fragile.
- M4-LO4: Students will be able to explain that profit and cash answer different questions (a profitable season can leave you with less cash), and sort every cash event into three buckets — Operating, Investing, Financing — whose totals tie out to the change in Cash.
- M4-LO5: Students will be able to reconcile net income to operating cash item by item (receivable, customer deposit, depreciation, card payoff) and explain that the income statement times things to the work while the Cash list times things to the cash — two honest clocks.
- M4-LO6: Students will be able to describe articulation (the statements tie out): a balance sheet cannot show a new number without the story filed beside it, and the difference travels through the income statement, the statement of changes in Members' Capital, and the statement of cash flows.
- M4-LO7: Students will be able to distinguish a back-of-the-envelope projection (an expectation, not a record) from the financial statements (which report what happened), and compute free cash flow from a napkin projection.
