## 1.1 — Drawing the Entity Boundary

**Light Bulb ideas**

- **"Look at what you just did"** — Pulled items from both home and the rented space; the box isn't "stuff in the space" or "tools," it's something else.
- **"The line you drew"** — The line is about whether each thing is part of the repair work, not where it sits, what it's used for, or who owns it.
- **"The deeper point"** — The first thing anyone running an activity must do is decide what belongs inside it and what stays outside.

**What you discovered**

- A little success made it impossible to keep the repair work in your head.
- By sorting your items, you drew a line: related to the repair work, or not.
- That line is not about where things are or what they are used for — it is about whether they are part of the repair work.

## 1.2 — Valuing the Things List

## 1.3 — Building Assets and Liabilities

**Light Bulb ideas**

- **"Not just cash"** — Cash doesn't always have to change hands for something to be recorded; what matters is whether something happened that changes what's tied up or still owed. Smith's upfront payment created an obligation; Jones only talked, so no obligation yet.
- **"Something You Still Have"** — The bank statement showed $1,300 going out, but the lease shows $650 for May and $650 is still useful after May — a present right to use the workspace later.
- **"The deeper point"** — A list of cash moving in and out is not the same as a list of what's tied up and what's still owed; accounting records are created by interpreting what transactions mean, not by copying cash transactions.

**What you discovered**

- Your personal bank statement mixed repair and personal activity; you had to separate the repair-related rows.
- The Cash sheet showed money that moved for repair work in May; net cash flow was −$160, but that alone didn't tell whether May was good or bad.
- Not all money coming in is yours to keep — Smith's $220 upfront created a promise to deliver the frame.
- Jones only talked about a frame; nothing changed what's tied up or owed, so Jones stayed off the Promises list.
- The card's $2,260 ending balance had only $2,030 still owed because of the repair work.
- Not all money going out is gone — the $1,300 rent was $650 for May and $650 for the final month (a present right to use the workspace later).
- Your informal lists now have accounting names: Assets ($3,410) and Liabilities ($2,250).

## 1.4 — The Reasons Tab and the Match-or-Reason Rule

**Light Bulb ideas**

- **"Most reasons show up in two rows"** — Same event, two changes; the pairing appeared once reasons were written next to the changes.
- **"The crossed-out ones just shuffled"** — Matched pairs moved two lines by the same amount, so the difference didn't change; the leftovers are what moved the difference.
- **"Two ways the difference grew"** — Contributed is what you funded from outside; Generated is what the work produced.
- **"Done: Equipment"** — Ten Equipment changes matched (card or Cash); three had no match (gear from home), now on the Reasons tab.
- **"Done: Computer equipment"** — The laptop had no match; the difference moved $610, only the Reasons tab says why.
- **"Done: Fixtures"** — Four asset lines matched one $750 card charge; matches live at the transaction level.
- **"Done: Supplies"** — Every part bought matched the card; every part used matched nothing, so $300 left the shelf and the difference went down.
- **"Done: Right of Use"** — Paying $1,300 bought a right (matched); May's month used matched nothing, so the difference moved down $650.
- **"Same rule, forward in time"** — Cash up $220 and promise up $220 matched; when Smith's frame is delivered, the promise drops with nothing moving — it will need a reason.
- **"The ledger doesn't reach the sheet"** — Cash rows sum to −$160, yet the Cash line reads $0; the $160 came from outside, covered by the personal self.
- **"The reasons are the record of the difference"** — Every change got a match or a reason; matched pairs never moved the difference.
- **"Two kinds, coming up"** — Some reasons were put in from outside, the rest came from doing the work; that split is next.

**What you discovered**

- Every change in an Asset or a Liability gets either a match or a reason; the Reasons tab holds the changes that had no match, each with its why.
- Tallying the Cash list forced a discovery: flows came out −$160, yet the bank account never went negative — your personal self covered the difference.
- Most changes came with a match — the other line of the same transaction; it showed up on its own.
- A matched pair never budges the difference — sometimes an Asset and a Liability move together, sometimes one Asset goes up and another down.
- The reasons that move the difference are two plain kinds: what you contributed and what the work generated.

## 1.5 — The Accounting Identity and Double-Entry

**Light Bulb ideas**

- **"The difference is d(NET)"** — Assets − Liabilities = $1,160; the difference is fully determined by the two lists.
- **"They match — and they always will"** — Every change got a match or a reason, so the reasons must add up to the difference — by construction, not coincidence.
- **"This page is the balance sheet"** — Built around the identity, not assembled to satisfy one; the difference follows by definition.
- **"The records show less than there is"** — Everything earned its place with evidence; skill, regulars, and reputation have no receipt; the difference is the gap between two recorded lists, not a net-worth figure.
- **"The boundary is the work; the name follows"** — Draw the line in the wrong place and every number after it is answering the wrong question.
- **"You invented the system. Now you have its names."** — Identify the entity, list Assets and Liabilities, the difference follows by construction — the accounting identity, on a balance sheet, maintained by double-entry.

**What you discovered**

- You drew a boundary for a purpose — that's the entity; you made it, you didn't find it.
- Recorded Assets minus recorded Liabilities is a definitional difference — not a pile of cash, and not a net-worth figure: the records leave real things out.
- A − L is the accounting identity — it holds by construction, and the page that lays it out is the balance sheet.
- The difference grows two ways — contributed and generated — what to call it depends on the kind of entity, a later story.
- Every change has two sides: double-entry, how every number on every statement gets there.

## 2.1 — The Book Boundary vs. the Legal Boundary

**Light Bulb ideas**

- **"Only the Books See the Boundary"** — Only your books treat the bike-repair role as separate; the bank, credit-card company, Smith, and the law see one undivided you; the separation is real in the books but informal.
- **"The second line"** — The boundary in your books is an accounting boundary; what comes next is formalizing it with a legal line; the order is always: first recognize, then formalize.
- **"The deeper point"** — You drew one boundary (accounting) that lets you track what's tied up, owed, and why the difference changed; what you don't have yet is a second boundary (legal).

**What you discovered**

- The boundary in your books is real but informal; it does not have legal force.
- Your books treat the bike-repair role as separate; the bank, credit-card company, customer, and law see one undivided you.
- A liability policy can help pay a claim but does not create a legal boundary between the repair work and your personal life.
- To create that second boundary, you need a legal line; one common form is an LLC.

## 2.2 — Filing the Certificate of Organization

**Light Bulb ideas**

- **"Certificate of Organization Filed"** — The State accepts the filing; the LLC is now a legal entity — a "legal person" that can own things, owe debts, sign contracts, and be sued in its own name.
- **"Legal Change, Not an Economic Change"** — The LLC now exists, but the filing didn't change the balance sheet; recorded assets, liabilities, and the difference are still the same.
- **"The deeper point"** — The Certificate gave the repair work a legal body but didn't create the repair work; two kinds of separation — accounting boundary and legal body — related but not the same.

**What you discovered**

- A legal entity comes into existence through a filing; once the State accepts the Certificate, a new "legal person" exists.
- The filing changed legal status but not the operation: same tools, same customers, same work today.
- The filing did not change the balance sheet; recorded assets, liabilities, and the difference are still the same.
- The legal body is new; the accounting boundary you drew in Module 1 came first.
- The LLC carries your name but is now legally separate from you.

## 2.3 — The Operating Agreement and the Keystone

**Light Bulb ideas**

- **"A draft, on purpose"** — The agreement is complete except Schedule A; you can't honestly fill it in until the contributing is done.
- **"Not one number changed"** — The same balance sheet is now the Company's, legally contributed in; "the difference" became Member's Capital (Contributed $990 + Generated $170 = $1,160).
- **"The wall"** — Limited liability is a legal fact — it changes who can be pursued, not what the records show; the balance sheet didn't move, the exposure did.
- **"The keystone"** — Accounting-entity ≠ legal-entity; the accounting entity came first and can exist without any legal body; the LLC is a legal wrapper placed around it.
- **"The deeper point"** — The same machine runs all the way up; a giant company draws one accounting boundary around dozens of separate legal companies.

**What you discovered**

- You wrote your own Operating Agreement — City Cycle's was only an example.
- It stays a draft until Schedule A is complete (the contributing isn't finished).
- The whole business was contributed in at book carryover — not one number changed; "the difference" became Member's Capital ($1,160).
- Limited liability put a wall between the Company's debts and your personal life — who can be pursued, not what the records show.
- The keystone: the accounting entity (since 1.1) is not the legal entity (days old); a legal body was wrapped around a boundary that already existed.

## 2.4 — The Entity's Own EIN and Accounts

**Light Bulb ideas**

- **"It all ties out"** — Each co-mingled account split into a business half and a personal half; the only new thing to the LLC is the $1,300 capital contribution.
- **"Two signatures, one hand"** — Signed twice: once for the Company (as manager), once as yourself (Sole Member); the two roles are now legal and literal.
- **"The schedule equals the sheet"** — You built Schedule A line for line from the Company's books; that's why it agrees exactly with the opening balance sheet.
- **"The one idea to keep"** — The accounting boundary and the legal boundary are different lines, drawn for different reasons; the same machine runs all the way up.

**What you discovered**

## 3.2 — Building the Income Statement

**Light Bulb ideas**

- **"A true list, but not yet an answer"** — The GENERATED pile never resets and is jumbled; no row belongs to "this season" and deliveries/used-up are scattered job by job.
- **"Two piles, two totals"** — Every season reason sorts into DELIVERED or USED UP; line them up and the difference is how the season went.
- **"It counts when the work is delivered, not when the cash comes"** — DELIVERED records the work going out the door; Ridgeline's delivery counts now and the cash follows on its own schedule; a receivable appears.
- **"The work is what counts"** — Ridgeline (work now, cash later) and Smith (cash earlier, work now) both belong in the pile now; the pile follows the work whichever way the cash runs.
- **"Revenue and Expenses"** — You rediscovered Revenue and Expenses, the two temporary accounts every business keeps period by period.
- **"Two period tabs, one continuous trail"** — Assets, Liabilities, and Reasons carry on; Revenue and Expenses fill for this season and will close when it does.
- **"The statement, but not the bottom line — yet"** — Revenue minus Expenses is Net income; the shape is right but one expense (depreciation) is still missing.
- **"The income statement explains a balance-sheet move"** — Net income is the amount added to GENERATED; the $600 draw is WITHDRAWN, a distribution not an expense.

## 3.3 — Depreciation and the Bottom Line

**Light Bulb ideas**

- **"Two kinds counted, one missing"** — Parts is directly observed use; Rent is time-lapsed; wear on tools is real but nothing to watch leave and no month that expires.
- **"An allocation, openly chosen"** — The $200 isn't a reading of anything; it's the season's share of a cost, set by a rule someone chose.
- **"Carrying value is a path, not a price"** — Cost minus depreciation charged so far tells where the number has been, not what it would fetch; a fully depreciated tool may still be worth something.
- **"How the season went — answered"** — Revenue $6,020 − Expenses $3,380 = Net income $2,640; the answer the jumbled pile couldn't give.
- **"The deeper point"** — One period question is answered by one statement from your own records, and sitting openly inside it is a chosen convention: depreciation.

**What you discovered**

- The shop ran its first season; the Reasons trail kept growing — one long true list.
- That one list could not answer "how did the season go?" — a pile of true rows is not a period's story.
- Sorting the season's rows — DELIVERED and USED UP — let them graduate to Revenue and Expenses tabs.
- Revenue follows the work, not the cash — Ridgeline's unpaid delivery stayed on the Revenue tab because the work was done.
- Three kinds of used-up: parts that meter off a shelf, rent that lapses with the calendar, and tools/laptop wear — depreciation, an allocation by an openly chosen convention.
- Carrying value is a path the records walk, not a price: cost minus depreciation charged so far; book and market need not agree.
- The income statement: Revenue $6,020 − Expenses $3,380 = Net income $2,640.
