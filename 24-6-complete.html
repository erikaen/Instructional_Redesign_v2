What You Built Across Module 2
  • You felt the gap: you recognized your bike-repair role in your books, but the bank, the credit-card company, Smith, and the law saw one undivided you (2.1).
  • You formalized the role — a CT Certificate of Organization brought a legal body into existence (2.2).
  • You drafted the Operating Agreement, contributed to the business at book amounts, and named the keystone: accounting-entity ≠ legal-entity — Schedule A left blank until the contributing was done (2.3).
  • You gave the entity its own EIN, account, and card — ending the co-mingling; the entity is a counterparty in its own right, able to owe and be owed in its own name (2.4).
  • Then you completed Schedule A from the Company’s books, line for line, and signed — once for the Company, once as yourself. The only move that touched the books all module was your $1,300 contribution (Cash 0 → 1,300; member’s capital 990 → 2,290).
The one idea to keep

The accounting boundary and the legal boundary are different lines, drawn for different reasons — you recognized the role first (Module 1, in the books), then formalized it (this module, at the state). The same machine runs all the way up: a giant company draws one accounting boundary around dozens of separate legal companies. Yours is one accounting entity that simply predates its single legal one.
