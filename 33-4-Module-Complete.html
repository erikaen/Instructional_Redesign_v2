What You Discovered
  • The books began as one continuous list. You chose the dates of interest — June 1, when the LLC’s books opened, and August 31, today — then added ruled, dated subtotal lines. Excel did the sums.
  • The June 1 lines tied to the June 1 balance sheet already in the workbook. Accounts Receivable and WITHDRAWN received no June 1 line because those accounts began during the season.
  • You read the GENERATED rows between the dated lines and copied those season rows to a working sheet. The source rows stayed in GENERATED.
  • You sorted the working rows, recorded Ridgeline’s $300 Accounts Receivable, and grouped expenses by kind: Parts Used $1,200, Rent $1,950, and Depreciation $230.
  • Depreciation supplied the reason no transaction had written. The $160, $40, and $30 details reduced the asset amounts and became one $230 expense line.
  • You formatted the season’s GENERATED rows on their own Income Statement tab: Revenue $6,020 − Total expenses $3,380 = Net income $2,640.
  • Net income $2,640 tells the season as a statement. The student-drawn GENERATED lines tell the same season as a change: $2,810 on August 31 minus $170 on June 1.
  • Carrying value is cost minus depreciation charged so far. It follows a path in the records; it is not a market price.
The deeper point

The books just flow as one continuous list. You purposefully divide that list at dates that matter, and the ruled lines mark the boundaries. The income statement sorts and formats the rows between those boundaries; depreciation adds an openly chosen allocation where no transaction supplied a reason.

Coming Next
