What You Discovered
  • The shop ran its first season, and the Reasons trail kept growing — one long true list of everything that happened.
  • That one list could not answer how did the season go? A pile of true rows is not the same as a period’s story.
  • Sorting the season’s rows — work DELIVERED and things USED UP — let them graduate to their own Revenue and Expenses tabs.
  • Revenue follows the work, not the cash. Smith’s frame held: Ridgeline’s unpaid delivery stayed on the Revenue tab because the work was done, and the receivable showed up there.
  • Three kinds of used-up surfaced: parts that meter off a shelf you can watch, rent that lapses with the calendar, and the kind that cannot be metered — tools, fixtures, and the laptop. That third kind is depreciation: an allocation by an openly chosen convention, not a reading.
  • Carrying value is a path the records walk, not a price: cost minus the depreciation charged so far. Book and market need not agree.
  • The income statement read straight off the two tabs: Revenue $6,020 − Expenses $3,380 = Net income $2,640.
The deeper point

One period question — how did the season go? — answered by one statement built from your own records. And sitting openly inside it is a chosen convention: depreciation, a share of cost assigned by a rule, not a reading. The records answer, and they show the convention they used.

Coming Next

A season that plainly went well — and the Cash line reads $620 where the account opened with $1,300. Both true, and neither one yet explained. And the difference between Assets and Liabilities — Members’ Capital, $2,460 when the season began — has moved, with nothing so far telling the story of how.

So the records have a season behind them, and an open question in front of them. Someone is about to look at the shop from the outside and decide whether to put money in — and the records will have to speak for themselves.
