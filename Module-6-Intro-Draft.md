## The turn (near-student voice)

Module 5 took the map you built in the bike shop and held it up against a real,
complex company — **CVS Health**. The map applied. CVS is owned by
shareholders; its Reasons side carried the label *shareholders' equity*; and the
whole architecture — two balance sheets with three explanatory statements
between them — held at the scale of a $250-billion company.

Module 6 holds the same map against a different kind of entity: **Yale
University**. Yale is a *nonprofit* — a not-for-profit corporation that
**nobody owns**. There are no shares, no shareholders, no dividends, and no
one to whom a residual could ever be paid out. Hold that fact next to the map
and a natural question follows: if the whole right side of the balance sheet was
about owners and their claims, what happens to it when there are no owners?

The answer is the module. The map still applies — Assets minus Liabilities still
computes to a difference, the difference still needs explaining, and the
explaining statements still connect one balance sheet to the next. But the
*names* change, the residual gets classified a different way, and the nonprofit
world adds its own real complications. The map survives contact with a company
whose owners are absent — and the details are where the accounting lives.

## What CVS confirmed (near-student voice — the recap)

Before Yale, it is worth naming what CVS *confirmed* about the map — the claims
that survived contact with a real company. Each ties to what you saw on the
Module 5 walk (the 53-x pages).

1. **The identity holds at any scale.** On CVS's balance sheets, recorded assets
   minus recorded liabilities equalled the residual exactly, at both dates:
   $253,538 − $178,156 = $75,382 (2025) and $253,215 − $177,485 = $75,730
   (2024), in millions. A − L = the residual, on a company a hundred thousand
   times the size of the bike shop (53-1).

2. **Cash gets its own explanation — read the labels.** CVS's balance-sheet Cash
   line barely moved ($8,586 → $8,453, down $133M) against roughly $400 billion
   of revenue. The Cash Flow Statement explained the change — but only once you
   read the labels: the balance sheet reports "Cash and cash equivalents," while
   the Cash Flow Statement reconciles a broader figure that includes restricted
   cash ($8,884 → $8,712, down $172M). Each statement explains a change defined
   by its own scope (53-2, 53-3).

3. **The residual changes only through the statements.** CVS's shareholders'
   equity went *down* $348M ($75,730 → $75,382) in a year it earned positive net
   income — and the Statement of Shareholders' Equity traced every mover:
   contributed +$769M, earned & retained −$1,115M, noncontrolling interests
   −$2M, summing to the −$348M (53-4). The residual cannot jump to a new balance;
   it must show the path.

4. **The income statement explains the generated part.** Behind the single "Net
   income" mover of $1,768M ($1,728M to CVS shareholders + $40M to
   noncontrolling interests) sat a $402-billion year of operations; add other
   comprehensive income and comprehensive income reconciled ($1,768 + $526 =
   $2,294M attributable to CVS) (53-5).

5. **The whole architecture is one connected system.** Two balance sheets, three
   explanatory statements between them, every change accounted for (53-6).

6. **Restrictive recognition still draws its line.** CVS's own brand — decisive
   to its business — is nowhere on CVS's balance sheet, off the books by the same
   convention that kept the bike shop's owner-skill and customer base off its
   ledger. Recorded ≠ everything the entity has.

The map, in one line: **A − L = a residual; a series of balance sheets connected
by statements that explain every change in it; and a recording convention that
draws a restrictive line around what gets in.** All six claims are about to hold
again — for an entity with no owners at all.

## What changes when nobody owns the place (near-student voice)

Everything that changes traces back to one fact: **no owners.** Walk it through
in the order the map is built.

1. **The identity is untouched.** Yale's balance sheet still sets recorded assets
   against recorded liabilities, and the difference still falls out of the
   arithmetic. On Yale's FY2025 statements: $59,043 − $9,672 = $49,371 (millions),
   and it held the year before too ($55,315 − $9,278 = $46,037). The residual is
   still the definitional difference between recorded assets and recorded
   liabilities — no more, no less.

2. **The residual gets a new name, for a real reason.** The label *equities* (and
   *shareholders' equity*) arose in a for-profit context where ownership exists.
   Yale has no owners, so that naming convention does not fit. The difference is
   instead called **Net Assets**. The change is in the *name*, not in the nature
   of the thing: Net Assets is still the definitional residual, not a pool of
   cash and not anyone's ownership claim. What it *does* track is different — see
   the next point.

3. **The residual is classified by donor restriction, not by source.** In the
   bike shop and at CVS, the residual was split by where it came from —
   *contributed* by owners versus *generated* by operations. A nonprofit has no
   owner contributions, so that split does not apply. Instead, Net Assets is
   split into two categories that track the status of **donor restrictions**:
   - **Net Assets Without Donor Restrictions** — contributions that never carried
     a donor restriction, or that did and have since had the restriction
     satisfied.
   - **Net Assets With Donor Restrictions** — contributions that came in with a
     donor-imposed purpose or time restriction that has not yet been met.

   At Yale, most of the residual is restricted: $38,609M with donor restrictions
   against $10,762M without — about 78% of net assets bound by donor intent.

4. **Only donors create binding restrictions.** This is the subtle, load-bearing
   rule. Yale's *board* can designate resources for a purpose — say, funds it
   intends to treat as endowment — but a board designation is a statement of
   intent the board can change at any meeting. It is **not** a restriction, and
   it stays in *Without Donor Restrictions*. Only an outside donor can impose a
   binding constraint that lands a dollar in the *With* category.

5. **The statements are renamed — and there are only two explaining ones, not
   three.** The balance sheet is called the **Statement of Financial Position**
   (SFP). The income statement's job is done by the **Statement of Activities**,
   whose bottom line is **change in net assets**, not net income — and which does
   the work of *both* CVS's income statement *and* its statement of changes in
   equity, because for a nonprofit every change in net assets flows through this
   one statement. The cash story is still told by a **Statement of Cash Flows**.
   So Yale's architecture is two balance sheets with **two** explanatory
   statements between them, where CVS had three.

6. **Restrictions release — a move with no for-profit equivalent.** When a
   donor's purpose or time restriction is finally met, the dollars are
   *reclassified* from *With Donor Restrictions* to *Without* — the **release
   from restriction**. No new resources arrive; total net assets do not change;
   the amount simply moves from one category to the other. Yale reclassified
   $1,615M this way in FY2025. This move exists only because the residual is
   split by restriction status in the first place.

7. **The endowment is the elephant in the room.** Yale holds $49,708M of
   investments at fair value — the endowment — which dominates the $59-billion
   asset side. Its return, its spending allocation, and the contributions that
   feed it drive most of what the Statement of Activities reports. The
   endowment's mechanics (a spending rule, a total-return concept) are a
   nonprofit-specific complication we will meet at an awareness level, not master.

8. **A total is not a spending account.** Large Net Assets do not mean large
   spendable resources. Net Assets is the residual of the identity, not a pile of
   cash; the resources behind it can be tied up in buildings, or locked inside a
   donor-restricted endowment. The question "how much can Yale actually spend
   soon?" is an *asset-side, liquidity* question, answered in a required
   footnote — where Yale reports about $5,029M of financial assets available
   within one year, against $59 billion of total assets. Read the total for what
   it is: a classification of the residual, not a bank balance.

> *Instructor-side note (not for the page): every claim above is grounded in the
> `nfp-accounting-pedagogy` skill — the required Net-Assets sequence (identity →
> name → why the name differs → with/without structure → the board-designation
> subtlety → the liquidity consequence), releases-as-reclassification, and
> liquidity-is-asset-side. NFP mechanics are not freelanced.*

## Where we are going: Yale University (near-student voice)

Yale's FY2025 financial report (for the year ended June 30, 2025) is the map
made real for a nonprofit. What the walk will show:

- **The Statement of Financial Position.** $59,043M of recorded assets, $9,672M
  of recorded liabilities, $49,371M of net assets — split $10,762M without donor
  restrictions and $38,609M with. And, as at CVS, a hard look at **what the
  statement does NOT show**: Yale's collections (the Beinecke, the Art Gallery,
  the Peabody specimens) sit at billions in fair value and *zero* on the books —
  not expensed, not recorded anywhere — a convention (ASC 958-360), not an
  oversight. The Yale name and its faculty are off the books for the same reason
  CVS's brand is.

- **The cash question, again — with a nonprofit twist.** Balance-sheet cash fell
  $293M ($1,058 → $765) while the Cash Flow Statement reports a $380M decrease —
  a label difference (cash held inside the investment pool) reconciled in the
  footnotes. Same lesson as CVS's restricted cash: read the labels.

- **The net-assets question.** Net assets rose **$3,334M** in the year — but the
  Statement of Activities shows most of that increase is *restricted* (endowment
  return and donor gifts), and $1,615M of it is not new money at all but
  restrictions *releasing*. The bottom line is change in net assets, and its
  composition matters more than its size.

- **Two statements, not three.** The Statement of Activities does the combined
  work of an income statement and a statement of changes in equity; the Statement
  of Cash Flows tells the cash story. Between the two Statements of Financial
  Position, they explain the whole year.

These are examples, not the whole list — the endowment's spending rule, the
functional classification of expenses, the collections election each carry
further complication. As in Module 5: **the map survives contact with reality;
the details are where the expertise lives.**

## Draft expandable note: "About the name Net Assets" (near-student voice)

> **A note on the name — click to expand.**
>
> Module 5 warned you that the word *equity* promises more than the number can
> deliver: it is a definitional residual — assets minus liabilities — not a
> stake anyone could collect. A nonprofit makes the point unavoidable. Yale has
> *no owners*. There is no one whose stake this could be, no share to price, no
> distribution that could ever be paid. So the profession does not call the
> difference "equity" here at all; it calls it **Net Assets.**
>
> Do not let the plainer word fool you either. "Net Assets" can sound like "the
> assets, netted down" — a spendable amount. It is not. It is the same
> definitional residual: recorded assets minus recorded liabilities, whatever
> that difference happens to be. At Yale it is $49.4 billion — and most of it is
> either restricted by donors or tied up in the endowment and the buildings, not
> sitting in an account waiting to be spent.
>
> What the name *does* buy you is a better fit to the accountability structure.
> A company answers to owners, so its residual is split by who *put money in*
> versus what operations *generated*. A nonprofit answers to donors and to its
> mission, so its residual is split by whether donors have *restricted* the
> money and whether those restrictions have been *met*. Same residual; different
> question asked of it. Read "Net Assets" the way Module 5 taught you to read
> "Equities" — as a *classification of a computed difference*, never as a pot of
> money with your name on it.
