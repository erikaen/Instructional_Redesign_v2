Tutorial 1.4: Keeping the Reasons

Bicycle repair shop • ~15 min

Anna’s back, coffee in hand. Your workbook is open to the two lists that earned their names in your last sit-down: Assets (what you have — $3,410) and Liabilities (what you owe — $1,450). And off to the side, still its own tab: Cash — the repair flows you pulled out of the bank statement together, netting −$960 for the month.

She studies the tab strip for a moment, then taps the screen. “That Cash tab has been bugging me since we built it. It’s just… sitting there, off to the side.”

ANNA
“There’s your new Reasons tab — I opened it in a second window, side by side, so a reason always sits next to the change it explains. Now go down your Equipment list. Click an item and record how it came in — the receipt () is the clue. One receipt sometimes brought in several things: one reason covers them all.”
ANNA
“Next line down: Computer Equipment — the laptop you run the shop on. Same move as the tools: click it and record how it came in. There’s no store receipt for this one — open the to see how it was valued.”
ANNA
“Now Supplies — same gesture, but watch closely: a parts line isn’t one event. Each number here is what’s left after two things happened — parts came in, and some got used up. Click any part: two reasons live behind it.”
ANNA
“Same gesture for Fixtures — the desk, shelving, display rack, pegboard. Click any of them and record how it came in. All four rode in on a single invoice, so one reason covers the lot — the receipt () is right there if you want to check.”
ANNA
“Next: Right of Use — the rent you paid ahead. Same as the parts: this line’s number is what’s left after two things happened. Click it — two reasons. But watch the second one; it isn’t the same kind of number as the parts.”
ANNA
“Flip to Liabilities — what you owe. I expanded the card so you can see the statement’s rows — the card company’s own record. Now read the first charge… Cyclesport, May 5, $300. You’ve seen that event before. Work down the list: if a reason already explains a row, find it; if a row is truly new, write it.”
Tutorial 1.4 complete
What You Discovered
  • You kept a Reasons tab — one row for the why behind each change to a list.
  • Most reasons came in pairs — same event, two changes. You didn’t arrange that; it showed up on its own.
  • The matched pairs just shuffle — have and owe move together, so your net doesn’t budge.
  • The reasons that move your net are two plain kinds: what you contributed and what the work generated.
  • The −$960 Cash line was the work showing it had drawn more out than it brought in — covered by your personal self. A reason explained what no amount could.
