Activity 2.1: The Two You’s

Module 2 · Getting Formal · ~10 min

The repair work has grown. More gear, more cash through the door, more customers than the month you started. Your books — Bike-Repair.xlsx — have kept the operation separate from your personal life since the very first week.

Then Maria, your insurance agent, calls back — with a question that has nothing to do with property, and everything to do with where the repair work ends and you begin.

What You’ll Work Out
  • What Maria is really warning you about — and why a policy alone won’t fix it.
  • Who, besides you, actually sees the repair work as separate from your own pocket.
  • What it would take to make that separation more than a habit.
MARIA
“Your property’s well covered. But there’s something the property policy never touched — and the bigger you get, the more it nags at me. Liability.”

“Say a bike you repaired fails on the road, and Smith — your customer — gets hurt. Smith doesn’t sue ‘the repair work.’ Smith sues you.”

That stopped you — because in your books, the repair work and your personal life are clearly two different things. You’ve kept them apart since day one: sorting that one co-mingled card by purpose every month, keeping a separate list, running a net. On paper, there are two of you. So why could Smith’s claim cross over?

Tap each one. Who treats the repair work as separate from you?

So there have been two of you all along — but only in a way you’ve kept by hand. The person, and the operation. Same head, two hats.

Your books drew one line. What would it take for the bank, the customer, and the law to see two — the way your books do?

The line you drew isn’t the only line
What You Worked Out
  • The separation you’ve kept in your books since 1.1 is real — but informal, with no legal force.
  • To you it’s two things; to the bank, the customer, and the law, it’s one person.
  • A liability policy pays a claim. It does not build a wall between the repair work and your personal life.
  • That wall is a different kind of line — a legal one — and it has a name: an LLC.
The deeper point

You’ve been keeping two you’s apart by hand. What you don’t have yet is a second line — a legal one — beside the line you drew in your books back in 1.1. They’re two different lines, drawn for two different reasons. Next, you’ll draw the legal one.
