## The turn (near-student voice)

This course has taught you two crafts. In Modules 1 through 4 you **built**
records from inside — a shoebox of receipts became a boundary, a workbook,
and finally a complete set of financial statements for Bike Repair LLC. In
Modules 5 and 6 you **read** statements from outside — a $250-billion company,
then a $59-billion university that nobody owns — and learned that the same map
survives contact with anything; only the names change with the entity.

Module 7 shows you what both crafts were handling all along: **one picture.**
The season you built, drawn as a single connected diagram — every account a
node, every recorded event an arrow between two of them — with the statements
you learned to build and to read revealed as *readings taken off that one
picture*. You will build the picture forward from the summer you know by
heart. Then the course will take the picture away, hand you nothing but the
statements, and ask you to work backward — the way every outside reader of
financial statements works every day.

The learning pattern stays what it has been since Module 5: objectives first,
then the material, then assessment.

## What you already hold (near-student voice — the parts the picture assembles)

Nothing in this module starts from zero. Six things you already own get
assembled into one machine:

1. **The accounting identity.** Assets minus Liabilities equals Member's
   Capital, at every date, by construction — the bike shop's June 1
   ($4,710 − $2,250 = $2,460) and August 31 ($4,500 − $0 = $4,500) both tie.
2. **Double-entry — two sides to every change.** The match-or-reason rule from
   Module 1: every change in the records pairs with a match or takes a reason.
   You have never seen a one-sided event, and you never will.
3. **The piles.** The Reasons side of the shop's records classifies every
   change in the difference by its source: Contributed, Generated, Withdrawn —
   kept from the first jot, never mixed.
4. **The walk.** Every statement you built walks a balance from beginning to
   ending — the Member's Capital walk ($2,460 → $4,500), the cash walk
   ($1,300 → $620). Beginning, plus what came in, minus what went out, ending.
5. **Articulation.** The statements tie to each other: every change on a
   balance sheet travels through a statement that explains it (Module 4's
   discovery, confirmed at scale in Modules 5 and 6).
6. **Read the labels.** A reported figure is defined by its own scope, and an
   aggregate can hide what composed it — the lesson that carried the CVS and
   Yale walks.

## What is new: the picture and its parts (near-student voice)

Four introductions, each named at first use on the page:

- **A node is an account's change.** Not the account's whole history — the
  season's change in it. Cash's node carries −$680; the Receivables node
  carries +$300. Nodes appear only when the season first touches them.
- **An arrow runs from the account that GAVE to the account that RECEIVED.**
  Every recorded event is one arrow with two ends — double-entry, drawn. The
  Parts shelf *gives up* $10 of parts for Dana's repair; Parts Used
  *receives* them. Cash *gives* $600; the owner's Withdrawn pile *receives*
  it. When more of the same thing happens, the arrow's total grows — one
  arrow, a bigger number — and that growing total is your first taste of what
  an aggregate is.
- **A dotted enclosure marks subparts.** Revenue and Expenses sit inside a
  dotted line around Generated: they are not new kinds of thing; they are
  Generated, in detail. The whole picture groups into the three regions you
  have used since Module 1: Assets, Liabilities, Reasons.
- **Every account walks — and the walk ties.** The identity ties the sheet;
  the walk ties each account: beginning, plus what came in, minus what went
  out, ending. On this module's pages you can *watch* it tie: the beginning
  balance sheet stands to the left of the picture, the ending balance sheet to
  the right, and the ending column's numbers move with every arrow until, when
  the season's last event posts, it becomes the August 31 balance sheet you
  built in Module 3.

## The bridge: where each idea appears in the picture

Working key (the page presents these as expandable items, in the 51-1/61-1
idiom):

| # | You own | In the picture |
|---|---------|----------------|
| 1 | The accounting identity | The flanking balance-sheet columns tie at every step — after every arrow, not just at the ends |
| 2 | Double-entry / match-or-reason | Every arrow has two ends. An arrow that stays inside Assets and Liabilities IS a match; an arrow that ends in the Reasons region IS a change that took a reason |
| 3 | The piles | The Reasons region holds Contributed, Generated (with Revenue and Expenses inside its dotted enclosure), and Withdrawn — the ending column shows all three pieces of Member's Capital |
| 4 | The walk | Each account's node (its change) plus its beginning-column number equals its ending-column number — visible per account, at every step |
| 5 | Articulation | The balance-sheet pair, the income statement, the Member's Capital walk, and the cash-flow statement are all readings off the one picture |
| 6 | Read the labels / aggregation | An arrow's total is a sum (watch it grow); one depreciation line ($230) hides a three-way split ($160/$40/$30); a net change merges gross flows an outsider cannot always un-merge |
| 7 | Restrictive recognition | The picture holds only what the records recognize — the owner's skill and the loyal customers have no nodes here, just as they had no lines there |

## Where we are going (near-student voice)

- **Build it forward.** The summer you know, drawn event by event: first the
  three events that stay entirely inside Assets and Liabilities — both ends on
  the sheets, matched, no Reason needed — then the events that open the
  Reasons region: Dana's $120, the season's other eighteen repairs growing the
  same arrow to $5,500, Ridgeline's $300 billed, Smith's $220 frame delivered,
  rent, the draw, and the gear giving up a season of its working lives. When
  the last arrow posts, the right-hand column *is* the August 31 balance
  sheet.
- **Read the statements off it.** The income statement ($6,020 − $3,380 =
  $2,640) and the Member's Capital walk ($2,460 + $2,640 − $600 = $4,500)
  filled in straight from the picture — then the cash question one level
  deeper: Cash's node opens into its three channels, you classify all seven
  cash arrows, and the profitable-but-poorer season ($2,640 earned, cash down
  $680) reconciles item by item.
- **Then lose the picture.** A prospective investor is considering buying into
  the shop — and outsiders never see the ledger, only statements, released a
  few at a time. From totals alone, then balance sheets, then the income
  statement, then the owner's replies, you will build the best lawful picture
  an outsider can build — tagging every arrow as *fact*, *inference under an
  assumption you can name*, or *open* — and recover numbers no single release
  states (the $600 the owner took; the $160 of the tools' share) using nothing
  but walks that must tie. At the end the shop's actual ledger fans open: your
  twelve lawful arrows stand for about fifty actual entries, and no statement
  at any release level could have given them to you.
- **And ask one last question.** The owner never took a wage — only the $600
  draw, which never touched the income statement. Move that one arrow — call
  the same $600 a wage — and net income falls to $2,040 while ending capital
  and cash do not move at all. Price the owner's bench time at a market wage
  and the profitable summer runs at a loss. Same summer, different pictures,
  all of them lawful. **Representation is a choice, and a reader must see
  through it.**

These are examples, not the whole list. The promise of the module: the
statements you can build and read are summaries of one picture — and knowing
the picture tells you exactly what a summary can and cannot say.

## Draft learning objectives (for Rick's edit — the M5/M6 pattern applied)

By the end of Module 7, the student will be able to:

- **M7-LO1:** Read a season drawn as a picture: nodes as account changes,
  arrows as give-and-receive pairs, dotted enclosures as subparts, and state
  in course language what any arrow records.
- **M7-LO2:** Explain how the balance-sheet pair, the income statement, the
  Member's Capital walk, and the cash-flow statement are each readings of the
  same picture — articulation, seen whole.
- **M7-LO3:** Walk any account — beginning, plus what came in, minus what went
  out, ending — and use a walk that must tie to recover an amount no
  statement reports directly.
- **M7-LO4:** Explain what aggregation hides: an arrow's total is a sum; a net
  change merges gross flows; a single reported line can stand for a split the
  records remember; statements are summaries an outside reader cannot always
  un-merge.
- **M7-LO5:** Build the best lawful picture from statements alone, tagging
  each arrow as fact, inference under a named assumption, or open — and say
  precisely what each new release added.
- **M7-LO6:** Show how one representational choice — the owner's draw against
  an owner's wage — changes net income and operating cash while leaving ending
  capital and total cash untouched, and explain why a reader of statements
  must look for such choices.

## Draft expandable note: "A note on the arrows" (near-student voice)

> **A note on the arrows — click to expand.**
>
> Every arrow in this module reads the same way: it runs **from the account
> that gave to the account that received**. The Parts shelf gives up parts;
> Parts Used receives them. Cash gives $600; the owner's Withdrawn pile
> receives it. The tools give a season of their working lives; Depreciation
> receives the season's share. Sometimes the reading takes a little
> imagination — when something is bought on credit, it is the liability
> account that gives: it hands the shop's promise to an outsider — but it
> always works.
>
> The profession has its own pair of names for the two ends of an arrow. They
> are five hundred years old, they are one of the first things every
> bookkeeping course teaches, and this course is deliberately not teaching
> them yet. Names are easiest to keep when you have already handled the thing
> they name — that has been this course's way since a summer's list of
> receipts went looking for a boundary. A later module teaches the
> profession's recording vocabulary all at once; when it does, the two ends of
> these arrows will already be old friends. For now, give and receive say
> everything the names would.
