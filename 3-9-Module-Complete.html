What You Discovered
  • Your personal bank statement mixed together two kinds of activity: the repair work and your personal activity. You first had to separate the repair-related rows from the personal rows.
  • The Cash sheet showed the money that moved in and out for the repair work during May. Net cash flow was −$960, but that number alone did not tell whether the repair work was better or worse off.
  • Not all money coming in is yours to keep. Smith paid $220 upfront for a custom frame that had not been built yet. That gave the repair work cash, but it also created a promise to deliver the frame.
  • Jones asked for a custom frame too, but you only talked about it. Nothing happened yet that affected what the repair work has or owes, so Jones stayed off the Promises list.
  • Your credit card had repair-related activity mixed with personal activity. The card’s ending balance was $1,460, but only $1,230 was still owed because of the repair work.
  • Not all money going out is gone. The $1,300 rent payment covered two things: $650 for using the workspace in May, and $650 paid ahead for the final month of the lease. That second $650 is a present right to use the workspace later.
  • Your informal lists now have accounting names: Assets ($3,410) and Liabilities ($1,450).
The deeper point

A list of cash moving in and out is not the same as a list of what the repair work has and owes. To build the accounting picture, you had to interpret the evidence: separate repair work from personal activity, tell completed work from work still owed, separate repair-related card debt from personal card spending, and split one rent payment into a used-up part and a future-benefit part.

That is the key idea: accounting records are not created by copying cash transactions. They are created by interpreting what those transactions mean.

Coming Next
