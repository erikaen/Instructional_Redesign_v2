Accounting-entity ≠ legal-entity
What You Worked Out
  • You wrote your own Operating Agreement — City Cycle’s (the one Anna set up) was only an example.
  • You signed it once for the Company and once as yourself — the two “you’s” made legal and literal.
  • The whole business was contributed in at book carryover — not one number changed; “what you put in” became members’ capital ($990 + $170 retained = $1,160).
  • Limited liability put a wall between the Company’s debts and your personal life — who can be pursued, not what the records show.
  • And the keystone: the accounting entity (since 1.1) is not the legal entity (days old). A legal body was wrapped around a boundary that already existed.
The deeper point

The same machine runs all the way up. A giant company draws one accounting boundary around dozens of separate legal companies; you drew one around an operation that, for now, has a single legal body younger than itself. The accounting line and the legal line are different lines, drawn for different reasons — and they rarely coincide.

The Company needs its own identity to the outside world — an EIN, its own bank account and card. The separation you’ve drawn by hand since 1.1 finally goes institutional.
