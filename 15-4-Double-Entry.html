Step back from the workbench for a second. None of what you built is new.

~3000 BCE. Scribes in Mesopotamia pressed wedge marks into clay: so much grain in the storehouse, so many sheep, who owes what at harvest. A list of things and a list of promises — your Assets and Liabilities tabs, five thousand years early. That half is ancient.

1494. A Franciscan friar, Luca Pacioli, writes down a method Italian merchants had used for two centuries: every change gets recorded on two sides. The pairing you stumbled into in your Reasons tab — where each change had a partner — is exactly it.

ANNA
“That pairing has a name: double-entry. Two sides to every change — no exceptions, not even the reasons. When Equipment went up and the Card went up with it, you saw both sides right there on the sheets: a match. When the laptop came in with nothing on the Card or in Cash to match it, it still had two sides — you just wrote the second one on the Reasons tab, because it belonged somewhere your sheets don’t have a name for yet. A match and a reason aren’t two different rules. They’re the same rule — every change gets a match or a reason — told twice, depending on where the second side landed. And it’s not a relic — every number on every financial statement produced today still gets there by double-entry. The identity and the balance sheet you built by hand are the same ones the largest companies in the world produce.”
