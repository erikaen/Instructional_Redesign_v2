What You Discovered
  • A season ran between two balance sheets, and Members’ Capital moved — but the two snapshots alone couldn’t say how. That gap was the reason the connecting statements existed at all.
  • Sorting the Reasons pile into DELIVERED and USED UP produced the Income Statement: Revenue $6,020 minus Expenses, following the work — not the cash. Ridgeline’s unpaid $300 stayed counted as revenue because the work was delivered, which is what surfaced the receivable.
  • Parts wear off a shelf you can watch (directly observed), and rent lapses with the calendar (time-lapsed) — but the tools, fixtures, and laptop do neither. Depreciation put a number on their wear anyway: an openly chosen allocation, $200 on tools & fixtures and $30 on the laptop, completing Expenses at $3,380 and net income at $2,640.
  • A profitable season still left less cash than opening day — $1,300 down to $620. The Statement of Cash Flows explained the gap: Operating +$320, Investing −$400 (the new tool), Financing −$600 (the draw), reconciling net income to what the Cash line actually shows.
  • Members’ Capital bridged from $2,460 to $4,500 through exactly two things: net income and the owner’s draw. Contributed never moved — no new money came in.
  • You performed the season’s first close: Revenue and Expenses emptied into GENERATED as one period row, and May’s long-open rows — waiting since Module 1 — filed the same way. Closing wasn’t a ritual; it was filing a finished period so the next one can start clean.
  • Laid side by side, the balance sheet and the three flow statements tied out — every change in Members’ Capital traveled through a statement. Accountants call this articulation.
The deeper point

A balance sheet is a photograph. On its own, it can only say where things stand — never how they got there. The four statements together are what let the records tell the whole story: two snapshots, and the connecting movie between them, each frame accounted for.

Nothing in that story ever needed an outside investor to justify it. The shop ran a season, kept its own books, and closed them — entirely on its own.

Coming Next

These statements are complete, and they tie out — but they were built for the shop’s own use. Suppose someone outside wanted to read them: would they see the whole picture? The skill at the bench that makes every repair possible, the name on Whalley Ave that keeps customers coming back — both plainly real, and neither appears anywhere in these four statements.
